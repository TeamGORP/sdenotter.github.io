INTRODUCTION

\r\n

The fight against financial crime is an ongoing priority for governments \r\n around the world. The ability of criminals and criminal organizations \r\n to use financial institutions to launder funds, along with the potential \r\n risk to their reputations, and ultimately to their safety and soundness, \r\n continues to be a concern for financial and other regulators. Over \r\n the past several years there has been extensive action in many countries \r\n to implement permanent measures to fight money laundering and terrorist \r\n financing. This action has been driven largely by the leadership \r\n of the FATF, of which Canada is a founding member.

\r\n

The FATF is the intergovernmental body that develops, monitors \r\n and evaluates country AML/ATF standards. These standards as set \r\n out in its 40 AML Recommendations and 9 ATF Recommendations establish \r\n a strong AML/ATF framework and permit a risk-based approach to the \r\n implementation of preventative measures.

\r\n

The Government of Canada, led by the Department of Finance, has \r\n established a private/public sector advisory committee to gather \r\n information, on an ongoing basis, on how Canada\xe2\x80\x99s AML/ATF regime \r\n can be continuously reviewed. The federal Government also implemented \r\n significant changes to the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR in 2007/2008 to ensure \r\n that the AML/ATF legislative framework is in line with international \r\n standards.

\r\n

OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s mandate includes supervising financial soundness and promoting \r\n the adoption, by management and Boards of FRFIs, of policies and \r\n procedures designed to mitigate risk. OSFI believes that the risk \r\n management outcomes identified in this Guideline will further reduce \r\n the susceptibility of FRFIs to being used by individuals or organizations \r\n to launder funds and fight terrorist financing, thereby reducing \r\n their exposure to damage to their reputation, a key asset in the \r\n financial services industry.

\r\n

To the extent possible, OSFI has aligned this Guideline to the \r\n framework of AML/ATF preventative measures set out in the FATF Recommendations. \r\n OSFI believes this will help focus attention on the principal goals \r\n of risk-based deterrence and detection.

Compliance with this Guideline

\r\n

FINTRAC is responsible for ensuring compliance with Part 1 of \r\n the PCMLTFA, and the PCMLTFR. These prescribe a compliance program \r\n with a risk-based component designed to ensure effective control \r\n over ML and TF risks.

\r\n

This Guideline does not create any new regulatory requirements. \r\n It is intended to assist FRFIs in identifying and complying with \r\n applicable AML/ATF requirements and measures contained in the PCMLTFA \r\n and the PCMLTFR. This Guideline is also aimed at helping institutions \r\n meet OSFI\'s governance and control expectations.

\r\n

Effective control over ML and TF risks, and related regulatory, \r\n operational and reputation risks, is essential.

\r\n

In order to achieve effective control, FRFIs will adopt different \r\n approaches to their AML/ATF programs that take into account the \r\n nature, scope, complexity and risk profile of their institution. \r\n FRFIs are expected to take into account the contents of this Guideline \r\n when implementing their AML/ATF programs. OSFI\'s AML/ATF assessment \r\n program, which aims to assist OSFI in evaluating the effectiveness \r\n of controls, takes the foregoing into consideration in the assessment \r\n of individual institutions.

\r\n

The OSFI Act enables OSFI and FINTRAC to exchange information \r\n on FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 compliance with Part 1 of the PCMLTFA. To this end, on \r\n June 14, 2004, OSFI and FINTRAC signed a Memorandum of Understanding \r\n for exchanging information. FRFIs should also be aware that in December \r\n 2008, FINTRAC will be able to impose administrative monetary penalties \r\n against its reporting entities, including FRFIs, for violations \r\n of prescribed provisions of the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR.

\r\n

FRFIs should note that FINTRAC, as the agency responsible for \r\n ensuring compliance with Part 1 of the PCMLTFA, and the PCMLTFR, \r\n publishes and maintains its own Guidelines on compliance with the \r\n PCMLTFA and the PCMLTFR. OSFI has made every effort not to duplicate \r\n in substance FINTRAC guidance. This Guideline should therefore be \r\n read in conjunction with FINTRAC\xe2\x80\x99s Guidelines, as appropriate. Where \r\n we do refer to matters touched on in FINTRAC\xe2\x80\x99s Guidelines, we have \r\n conformed references to those used by FINTRAC.

\r\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFit & Proper\xe2\x80\x9d requirements for significant \r\n owners, directors and senior officers of FRFIs

\r\n

The FATF Recommendations include measures to mitigate the risk \r\n that criminals and other inappropriate persons might take over ownership \r\n of, or unduly influence the management of, financial institutions. \r\n

\r\n

OSFI screens all persons who own or control, directly or indirectly, \r\n significant interests in FRFIs. This screening is done prior to \r\n the approval of a new FRFI and when ownership interests change. \r\n In addition, OSFI screens directors and senior officers who will \r\n be in place when a FRFI commences operations. However, OSFI seeks \r\n to rely on FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 internal processes for assessing the ongoing suitability \r\n and integrity of directors and senior officers who are appointed \r\n after the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s initial start up.

\r\n

OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s expectations of FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 internal processes for screening \r\n directors and senior officers post- authorization are set out in \r\n OSFI Guideline E-17 \xe2\x80\x9cBackground Checks on Directors and Senior Management \r\n of FREs\xe2\x80\x9d. A risk-based approach to assessing the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s own screening \r\n processes is applied by OSFI where warranted. Compliance with Guideline \r\n E-17 in pertinent respects will be included in OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s AML/ATF assessment \r\n methodology.

\r\n

Guidance on Designated Name Searching and \r\n Sanctions

\r\n

Certain provisions of the PCMLTFA and the Criminal Code give \r\n both FINTRAC and OSFI responsibility for dealing with issues related \r\n to the financing of terrorist activities.

\r\n

FINTRAC\xe2\x80\x99s objectives include the prevention, detection, and deterrence \r\n of the financing of terrorist activities, while OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s role is that \r\n of a central reporting channel for the aggregate reporting requirements \r\n outlined in subsection 83.11(2) of the Criminal Code.

\r\n

With respect to FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 terrorist property reporting obligations, \r\n OSFI posts on its Internet site (www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca) lists of terrorist \r\n individuals and organizations, and will continue to receive monthly \r\n reports from FRFIs on the findings of their continuous searching \r\n for and freezing of terrorist assets as required by the regulations \r\n under the United Nations Act or by subsection 83.11(1) \r\n of the Criminal Code in respect of designated entities. \r\n In addition, FINTRAC and a number of international organisations \r\n have published information related to terrorist financing activities. \r\n FINTRAC has also issued a guideline on Submitting Terrorist Property \r\n Reports.

\r\n

Over the past few years, Canada has implemented several new economic \r\n and anti-proliferation (of weapons of mass destruction) sanctions \r\n against a number of countries, entities and designated persons. \r\n In addition, the FATF has issued guidance documents on a number \r\n of these and related matters. The array of obligations imposed on \r\n FRFIs by the reporting requirements, sanctions and related procedural \r\n actions merits dealing with designated name searching, listings, \r\n reporting, economic and anti- proliferation sanctions in a separate \r\n Guideline. OSFI anticipates that this Guideline will be issued in \r\n 2009.

\r\n

Legislative Compliance Management

\r\n

The components of the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s AML/ATF program that are designed \r\n to comply with the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR should be incorporated into, \r\n or referenced by, the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s LCM framework. Although the chief compliance \r\n officer is responsible for the LCM framework generally (Guideline \r\n E-13: Legislative Compliance Management), the AML/ATF components \r\n of the LCM framework should be the responsibility of the CAMLO. \r\n

\r\n

AML/ATF Guidance Issued by FATF and International \r\n Supervisory Bodies

\r\n

The FATF, the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision and the International \r\n Association of Insurance Supervisors have each issued risk-based \r\n AML/ATF guidance directed at the financial sector. FRFIs should \r\n consult the appropriate guidance issued by these bodies for more \r\n information on risk assessment and effective controls.

\r\n

THE RISK BASED APPROACH IN THE CONTEXT OF \r\n THE PCMLTFA AND PCMLTFR

\r\n

The basic principle underpinning OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s Supervisory Framework \r\n is that FRFIs must develop and implement effective risk management \r\n controls to manage their exposure to financial risk and ultimately \r\n their financial stability and soundness.

\r\n

This Guideline aims to assist FRFIs in their development and implementation \r\n of effective AML/ATF controls to manage their exposure to ML and \r\n TF risks.

\r\n

The PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR prescribe various outcomes that FRFIs \r\n must achieve to detect and deter ML and TF. These outcomes are set \r\n out as regulatory requirements which, in the aggregate, form the \r\n compliance regime to be embedded in FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 AML/ATF programs. Examples \r\n of regulatory requirements include: the identification of clients; \r\n the appointment of a CAMLO; determining whether a client is a PEFP; \r\n the prohibition on dealing with shell banks. Some requirements feed \r\n into broader outcomes; others are themselves outcomes.

\r\n

In all cases, the manner in which these outcomes may be achieved \r\n is prescribed. Generally, there are three ways in which the PCMLTFA \r\n and the Regulations prescribe how an outcome is to be achieved: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • 1. By one or more Prescriptive Measures
    • \r\n
\r\n

In these situations, one or more measures are prescribed. All \r\n of the prescribed measures must be followed. An example is PEFP \r\n determination - if a client is determined to be a PEFP, certain \r\n prescribed measures must be taken.

\r\n
    \r\n
  • 2. By a choice of Prescriptive Measures
    • \r\n
\r\n

In these situations, a choice of alternative measures is prescribed. \r\n These measures offer FRFIs flexibility in achieving the prescribed \r\n outcome. Aside from selecting which option to choose, no other options \r\n or alternatives are available to the FRFI. Examples include prescribed \r\n types of acceptable identification documentation for individuals \r\n and prescribed sets of alternative measures for the identification \r\n of credit card clients in non-face-to face situations.

\r\n
    \r\n
  • 3. By Reasonable measures
    • \r\n
\r\n

In these situations, the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR allow FRFIs more \r\n flexibility to determine for themselves how to achieve the prescribed \r\n outcomes, provided that the measures chosen are \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d. To \r\n be reasonable, the measures used must achieve the prescribed outcome. \r\n An example is reasonable measures to determine the source of funds \r\n for certain high risk clients.

\r\n

This Guideline identifies measures that OSFI has found to be reasonable \r\n when applied effectively \xe2\x80\x93 i.e., when they achieve prescribed outcomes. \r\n The measures, which are drawn from a wide base of sources, including \r\n the FATF, should not be treated as checklists.

\r\n

As noted below, OSFI expects FRFIs to have AML/ATF programs in \r\n place that include measures which are not expressly addressed by \r\n the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR, but which are consistent with other OSFI \r\n Guidance and OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s Supervisory Framework. Examples include corporate \r\n governance measures to ensure appropriate oversight by the Board \r\n and effective measures for the self assessment of controls.

\r\n

AML/ATF PROGRAM

\r\n

The AML/ATF program is the key vehicle for establishing and maintaining \r\n effective control over ML and TF risks in all relevant areas of \r\n the FRFI enterprise.

\r\n

The following is a more detailed description of OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s expectations \r\n and prescribed content of the AML/ATF program:

\r\n

Principal Elements of AML/ATF Program

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that their AML/ATF programs include the following \r\n elements, each of which is expanded upon in this Guideline. Elements \r\n required by the PCMLTFA and the PCMLTFR are marked with an asterisk: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • \r\n

    Board and Senior Management Oversight, including *Reporting \r\n to Senior ManagementFootnote 1; \r\n

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    *An appropriate individual responsible for implementation \r\n of the programFootnote 2. \r\n See further, \xe2\x80\x9cCAMLO\xe2\x80\x9d;

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    *Assessment of inherent ML and TF risksFootnote 3. \r\n See further, \xe2\x80\x9cAssessment of Inherent Risks\xe2\x80\x9d;

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    *Written AML/ATF policies and procedures that are kept up \r\n to dateFootnote 4. See further, \r\n \xe2\x80\x9cControl Policies and Procedures\xe2\x80\x9d;

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    *Written ongoing training programFootnote 5. \r\n See further, \xe2\x80\x9cOngoing Training\xe2\x80\x9d;

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    Self Assessment of controls. See further, \xe2\x80\x9cSelf Assessment \r\n of Controls\xe2\x80\x9d; and

    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • \r\n

    *Effectiveness testingFootnote 6. \r\n See further, \xe2\x80\x9cEffectiveness Testing\xe2\x80\x9d.

    \r\n
    • \r\n
\r\n

Scope

\r\n

The AML/ATF program should implement a corporate standard of inherent \r\n risk assessment and risk control measures, across all relevant business \r\n areas of the FRFI.

\r\n

The formality and sophistication of the AML/ATF program should \r\n be commensurate with the size and complexity of the FRFI and its \r\n businesses. As a general principle, the corporate standard should \r\n be consistent with Canadian regulatory requirementsFootnote 7. \r\n The PCMLTFAFootnote 8 \r\n requires that standards consistent with s. 6, 6.1 and 9.6 of the \r\n PCMLTFA be applied in respect of wholly owned subsidiaries and branches \r\n in countries that are not members of the FATF where the laws of \r\n such countries permit it. FRFIs should ensure that unless there \r\n is an explicit prohibition, such standards are applied.

\r\n

FRFIs should notify OSFI if a country explicitly prohibits compliance \r\n with s. 9.7 or 9.8 of the PCMLTFA, to assist OSFI in analysing the \r\n situation in respect of that country.

\r\n

BOARD AND SENIOR MANAGEMENT OVERSIGHT

\r\n

Role of the Board

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that the Board has oversight accountability \r\n for approving policies and procedures and monitoring the effectiveness \r\n of the AML/ATF program on a regular basis. The Board\xe2\x80\x99s oversight \r\n practices in respect of the AML/ATF program should align with OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n Corporate Governance Guideline, in particular the section \xe2\x80\x9cThe Role \r\n of the Board in Risk Management.\xe2\x80\x9d

\r\n

The Board should ensure that its oversight accountability is documented. \r\n One reasonable way to accomplish this would be to have its oversight \r\n for the AML/ATF program documented in the mandate(s) of one or more \r\n committees of the Board.

\r\n

Reasonable measures to ensure that the Board is implementing an \r\n effective mandate could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Approval of the AML/ATF Program framework, including key AML/ATF \r\n policies;
    • \r\n
  • Receiving regular reports from the CAMLO and other senior officers \r\n on the design and operation of the AML/ATF program, including \r\n the adequacy of resources (people, data management systems and \r\n budget) supporting the program;
    • \r\n
  • Receiving regular reports from the Auditor on effectiveness \r\n testing; and
    • \r\n
  • Receiving sufficient briefing with respect to inherent risks \r\n and controls so as to have an adequate level of understanding \r\n about AML/ATF matters.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Role of Senior Management

\r\n

Senior Management\xe2\x80\x99s oversight responsibilities in respect of the \r\n AML/ATF program should align with OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s Corporate Governance Guideline, \r\n in particular the section \xe2\x80\x9cThe Relationship between the Board and \r\n Senior Management\xe2\x80\x9d. Senior Management should have responsibility \r\n and accountability for: directing day-to-day implementation and \r\n management of the AML/ATF program; ensuring that it is adequate \r\n to mitigate ML and TF risk; that it complies with the PCMLTFA and \r\n PCMLTFR as required; and that it is implemented effectively in all \r\n relevant business areas.

\r\n

Senior Management should ensure that:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • The CAMLO is appropriately qualified and has clear and documented \r\n authority and accountability for the design of the AML/ATF program; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • The CAMLO does not report to the Auditor or revenue-producing \r\n businesses, in order to avoid potential conflicts of responsibilities. \r\n In smaller FRFIs, where functional segregation may be difficult \r\n to achieve, compensating controls should be established to meet \r\n this goal. Consideration should be given to outsourcing the CAMLO \r\n function if compensating controls are not practical or possible. \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Qualified individuals have clear and documented responsibility \r\n and accountability for AML/ATF program implementation in all relevant \r\n business areas of operation, and sufficient resources to manage \r\n program implementation effectively;
    • \r\n
  • The CAMLO and the Auditor have adequate resources in terms \r\n of people, data management systems and budget to implement and \r\n administer the AML/ATF program requirements effectively and to \r\n offer objective opinions or advice to the Board and Senior Management; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • All significant recommendations in respect of AML/ATF program \r\n issues and controls made by the CAMLO, the Auditor and Senior \r\n Management are acted upon in a timely manner.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Reporting to Senior Management and the Board \r\n

\r\n

Senior Management should ensure they receive sufficient pertinent \r\n information from the CAMLO, the Auditor and other sources as appropriate, \r\n to enable them to ensure the overall adequacy and effectiveness \r\n of the AML/ATF program.

\r\n

The PCMLTFRFootnote 9 \r\n prescribe timing and content of written reports on effectiveness \r\n testing, including reporting on any updates made to AML/ATF policies \r\n and procedures and the status of the implementation of such updates. \r\n

\r\n

In larger, more complex, FRFIs, AML/ATF reports on effectiveness \r\n testing made at different times (for example, during audits of different \r\n business areas) should be collated and consolidated periodically. \r\n This will support the goal of assessing overall adequacy and effectiveness. \r\n

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that AML/ATF reporting to Senior Management \r\n and the Board by the CAMLO and by the Auditor is not unduly commingled, \r\n in order to differentiate the contents and purpose of the reporting. \r\n

\r\n

The reports from the CAMLO should include information about: the \r\n FRFI-wide scope of the assessment of inherent risks including: significant \r\n patterns or trends; the self assessment of controls and material \r\n changes thereto; and remedial action plans or recommendations, if \r\n any, with milestones and target dates for completion. Where appropriate, \r\n the CAMLO should draw conclusions, offer advice or make recommendations \r\n about the overall structure and scope of the AML/ATF program.

\r\n

CAMLO

\r\n

Introduction

\r\n

Whether or not the broader risk management structure of the FRFI \r\n is decentralized, responsibility for implementation of the enterprise \r\n AML/ATF program should be assigned to the CAMLO, who should be one \r\n person positioned centrally at an appropriate senior corporate level \r\n of the FRFI. For the purposes of this Guideline, FRFIs should treat \r\n the CAMLO as an independent oversight function as described in OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n Corporate Governance Guideline.

\r\n

The CAMLO is expected to be responsible both for the regulatory \r\n compliance component and the broader prudential \r\n risk management component of the AML/ATF program.

\r\n

Mandate

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that the CAMLO has clear and documented responsibility \r\n and accountability for AML/ATF program content, design and enterprise-wide \r\n implementation. In particular, the CAMLO\xe2\x80\x99s mandate should include \r\n accountability for:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • oversight of AML/ATF control activity in all relevant business \r\n areas for the purposes of establishing a reasonable threshold \r\n level of control consistency throughout the enterprise;
    • \r\n
  • keeping the AML/ATF program current relative to the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n identified inherent risks (clients and business relationships, \r\n products and delivery channels, geographic locations of activity \r\n and any other relevant factors);
    • \r\n
  • developing and implementing an assessment of inherent ML and \r\n TF risks, including but without prejudicing the generality of \r\n the foregoing, being satisfied that new product/service/business \r\n acquisition processes are subjected to timely inherent risk analysis \r\n and appropriate measures are developed to control identified risks. \r\n See further, \xe2\x80\x9cAssessment of Inherent Risks\xe2\x80\x9d;
    • \r\n
  • being satisfied that systems resources, including those required \r\n to identify and report suspicious transactions and suspicious \r\n attempted transactions, are sufficient in all relevant areas of \r\n the FRFI;
    • \r\n
  • developing and implementing a self assessment of controls; \r\n see further, \xe2\x80\x9cSelf Assessment of Controls\xe2\x80\x9d;
    • \r\n
  • written AML/ATF policies and procedures that are kept up to \r\n date and approved by a senior officer;
    • \r\n
  • written ongoing training programs for Senior Management, employees, \r\n agents and other persons authorized to act on the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s behalf; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • ensuring that the Auditor is aware of the requirement in the \r\n PCMLTFR for effectiveness testing of the AML/ATF program at least \r\n every two years;
    • \r\n
  • being satisfied that systems and other processes that generate \r\n information used in reports to Senior Management and the Board \r\n are adequate and appropriate, use reasonably consistent reporting \r\n criteria, and generate accurate information; and
    • \r\n
  • reporting to Senior Management and the Board pertinent information \r\n about AML/ATF program adequacy and issues.
    • \r\n
\r\n

If the CAMLO delegates or assigns duties to other individuals, \r\n or if the FRFI assigns some elements of the AML/ATF program to business \r\n areas that do not report to the CAMLO, the CAMLO should take reasonable \r\n measures to be satisfied that such elements are implemented satisfactorily. \r\n Reasonable measures to achieve this could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • where those responsible for filing STRs do not report to the \r\n CAMLO, being satisfied that threshold-based criteria are consistent, \r\n that reporting is accurate and timely, and ensuring the CAMLO \r\n receives regular summary reports on STR filings from such areas \r\n of the FRFI; and
    • \r\n
  • Establishing a management committee to coordinate the implementation \r\n of the AML/ATF program.
    • \r\n
\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that the CAMLO has:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • unfettered access to, and direct communications with, Senior \r\n Management and the Board; and
    • \r\n
  • unfettered access to all pertinent information, records and \r\n personnel throughout the FRFI.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Qualifications

\r\n

Responsibility for the implementation of the AML/ATF program requires \r\n that the CAMLO have a thorough working knowledge of ML/TF risks \r\n and controls in the FRFI and AML/ATF regulatory requirements; a \r\n broad knowledge of the operations of the FRFI; and appropriate professional \r\n qualifications, experience and strong leadership skills.

\r\n

Consideration should be given to these factors when FRFIs consider \r\n the seniority and reporting relationship of the CAMLO.

\r\n

ASSESSMENT OF INHERENT RISKS

\r\n

The PCMLTFAFootnote 10 \r\n requires that the compliance program include the development and \r\n application of policies and procedures to assess, in the course \r\n of a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s activities, the risk of a ML or a TF offence.

\r\n

The PCMLTFRFootnote 11 \r\n requires that the following categories of ML and TF risk be covered \r\n in a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of inherent risk:

\r\n
    \r\n
  1. the clients and business relationships of the FRFI;
    2. \r\n
  2. the products and delivery channels of the FRFI;
    3. \r\n
  3. the geographic location of the activities of the FRFI; and \r\n
    4. \r\n
  4. any other relevant factor.
    5. \r\n
\r\n

For the purposes of item (iv) above, FRFIs should take into account \r\n transaction risk factors, for example, structured or otherwise complex \r\n transactions, and factors that may fall into more than one of the \r\n other three categories.

\r\n

Assessment of inherent risks refers to a process that:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • identifies current and emerging ML and TF risks inherent in \r\n activities of the FRFI without reference to any controls over \r\n them and whether or not the activities in which they reside are \r\n considered material in dollar terms;
    • \r\n
  • assesses the relative seriousness of the identified risks; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • highlights the higher risks among them.
    • \r\n
\r\n

In considering ML and TF risks, consideration should be given \r\n to what, if any, pertinent changes have occurred since the assessment \r\n of inherent risks was last performed. Reasonable measures to accomplish \r\n this could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • consideration of factors that led to the filing of suspicious \r\n transaction reports and any patterns or trends in these; and
    • \r\n
  • consideration of external factors such as regulatory developments, \r\n ML or TF typologies and regulatory Notices and Advisories.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Regular assessment of inherent ML and TF risks enables FRFIs to \r\n tailor or adjust corporate control measures to identified risk, \r\n which in turn facilitates the allocation of more risk management \r\n resources to areas of greater vulnerability. See further, \xe2\x80\x9cSelf \r\n Assessment of Controls\xe2\x80\x9d below.

\r\n

The following sections discuss the methodology and desired outcomes \r\n of the process used to analyse inherent ML and TF risks.

\r\n

Methodology

\r\n

There is no single prescribed or universally used methodology \r\n for inherent ML/TF risk assessment. However, the methodology used \r\n should assess the risk of ML offences or TF offences across the \r\n FRFI and include the categories of risk identified in p. 71(1)(c) \r\n of the PCMLTFR.

\r\n

The outcome of the methodology should be a rational, well-organized \r\n and well-documented inherent risk analysis.

\r\n

Reasonable measures to include in the methodology used would include \r\n consideration of:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • the business lines and other operations of the FRFI;
    • \r\n
  • cross-border and international operations, if any, and linkages \r\n among these;
    • \r\n
  • typologies of how financial institutions have been abused; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • any other relevant information that is available to the FRFI. \r\n
    • \r\n
\r\n

Risk Categories

\r\n

Inherent risk assessment should address the different categories \r\n of risk exposure to ML and TF. The PCMLTFRFootnote 12 \r\n requires that inherent risk assessment address the following specific \r\n categories of risk. In each category, OSFI has indicated types of \r\n risks.

\r\n

Client Risk:

\r\n

This is risk associated with types of clients that buy or use \r\n the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s products and services. Categories of clients that may \r\n indicate a higher risk could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • politically exposed persons;
    • \r\n
  • clients conducting their business relationship or transactions \r\n in unusual circumstances, such as geographic distance from the \r\n FRFI for which there is no reasonable explanation;
    • \r\n
  • clients whose nature, structure or relationship make it difficult \r\n to identify the ultimate beneficial owner(s) of significant or \r\n controlling interests, including clients that are corporations \r\n with the ability to issue bearer shares;
    • \r\n
  • cash (and cash equivalent) intensive businesses including: \r\n
      \r\n
    • Money services businesses (for example, remittance houses, \r\n foreign exchange businesses, money transfer agents, bank note \r\n traders, cash couriers or other businesses offering money \r\n transfer or movement facilities);
      • \r\n
    • Casinos, betting and other gaming-related businesses;
      • \r\n
    • Businesses that, while not normally cash-intensive, generate \r\n substantial amounts of cash for certain lines of activity; \r\n and
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • charities and other non-profit organizations that are not monitored \r\n or supervised (for example, not registered with CRA).
    • \r\n
\r\n

Business relationship risk:

\r\n

This is risk associated with the client\xe2\x80\x99s stated purpose in dealing \r\n with the FRFI. Categories of business relationships that may indicate \r\n a higher risk could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • intermediary structures, such as holding companies, numbered \r\n companies or trusts, that have no apparent business purpose or \r\n that make beneficial owners difficult to identify;
    • \r\n
  • accountants, lawyers or other professionals holding commingled \r\n funds accounts where the beneficial ownership of the funds may \r\n be difficult to verify; and
    • \r\n
  • use of the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s products or services by clients of clients, \r\n for example, clients of correspondent banks.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Product/Service Risk:

\r\n

This is risk associated with FRFI products/services that enable \r\n clients to move funds. Categories of products and services that \r\n may indicate a higher risk could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • deposit-taking, especially cash, and insurance products that \r\n allow large one-time or regular payments, pre-payments or deposits, \r\n to be made and subsequently withdrawn from deposit or deposit-like \r\n accounts (for example, side accounts);
    • \r\n
  • \xe2\x80\x9cfree look\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccooling off\xe2\x80\x9d periods coupled with premium refunds, \r\n for example, in some life insurance products;
    • \r\n
  • cash values, early cash surrender and loan provisions, and provisions \r\n for deposit, accumulation and withdrawal of funds with relative \r\n ease and speed, for example, non- registered segregated funds; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • trade finance services where \r\n
      \r\n
    • the FRFI is not able to assess whether the values of goods \r\n or services being imported or exported are reasonable; or \r\n
      • \r\n
    • FRFIs confirm, advise or make payments under letters of \r\n credit for purposes of their clients\xe2\x80\x99 buying or selling goods \r\n internationally.
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • credit accounts in respect of which large credit balances are \r\n allowed to be maintained, for example, some credit and corporate \r\n card products;
    • \r\n
  • payable through accounts that permit clients of a foreign correspondent \r\n bank to draw drafts (or cheques) on Canadian-based accounts;
    • \r\n
  • lock boxes for the use of clients of foreign correspondent \r\n banks that permit such banks to collect payments due from their \r\n clients domiciled in Canada; and
    • \r\n
  • pouch services and similar international commercial payment \r\n services.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Delivery Channel Risk:

\r\n

This is risk associated with how FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 products/services are \r\n delivered to clients including services delivered to clients non-face-to-face. \r\n Categories of delivery channels that may indicate a higher risk \r\n could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • use of intermediaries or introducers (for example, mortgage \r\n and deposit agents and brokers), that may not be subject to AML/ATF \r\n laws and measures and who are not adequately supervised;
    • \r\n
  • the Internet, telephone and mail when used as a complete substitute \r\n for face to face interaction with the client in delivering banking \r\n services; and
    • \r\n
  • transfers payable upon presentation of identity (PUPIDs).
    • \r\n
\r\n

Geographic location risk:

\r\n

This is risk associated with places in which FRFI activities are \r\n carried out. Where FRFIs have subsidiaries or branches in such places, \r\n this may mitigate or elevate the risk. Categories of countries that \r\n indicate a higher risk include countries:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • subject to Canadian or other national sanctions, embargoes \r\n or similar measures, such as the Special Economic Measures \r\n Act or measures prescribed under the USA PATRIOT Act;
    • \r\n
  • subject to United Nations Security Council (UNSC) sanctions \r\n (in Canada, UNSC sanctions are applied by regulations issued under \r\n the United Nations Act);
    • \r\n
  • identified by credible sources as providing funding or support \r\n for terrorist activities or the proliferation of weapons of mass \r\n destruction;
    • \r\n
  • identified by credible sources as having significant levels \r\n of corruption or other criminal activity;
    • \r\n
  • that are not members of the FATF, and in particular, countries \r\n that are subject to monitoring by the FATF or otherwise identified \r\n by the FATF as lacking appropriate AML/ATF regulatory requirements; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • where legislation prohibits or unduly restricts access to client \r\n information by the CAMLO.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Other relevant factors:

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that they take any other relevant factors \r\n into consideration in an inherent risk assessment, including transaction \r\n risk factors and combinations of factors that may fall within more \r\n than one of the other three categories.

\r\n

Rating and Ranking

\r\n

An appropriate methodology should assign appropriate ML and TF \r\n risk levels to the pertinent activities of the FRFI and in so doing, \r\n identify the higher risks to which enhanced due diligence and ongoing \r\n monitoring must be applied.

\r\n

The criteria used for rating and ranking should have a rational \r\n basis in ML and TF risk and address ML and TF risk factors that \r\n are unique to specific business lines, areas and jurisdictions, \r\n and also more general risk factors.

\r\n

Finally, the methodology should enable FRFIs to comply with the \r\n regulatory requirement to identify higher risk clients activity \r\n Footnote 13for purposes \r\n of establishing a threshold level of enhanced due diligence that \r\n is appropriate in the circumstances. See \xe2\x80\x9cCustomer Due Diligence\xe2\x80\x9d \r\n below.

\r\n

Using Assessment of Inherent Risks as the \r\n Basis for Risk Controls

\r\n

Results of the assessment of inherent risks should inform the \r\n development of risk controls, and the allocation of resources, commensurate \r\n with levels of ML and TF risk in the enterprise.

\r\n

Certain risk control measures are prescribed by regulatory requirements. \r\n These cannot be qualified or bypassed by inherent risk assessments. \r\n They include, for example:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • client identification and ascertaining identity (subject to \r\n prescribed exemptions);
    • \r\n
  • determining under prescribed circumstances whether a client \r\n is a PEFP or is acting on behalf of a third party;
    • \r\n
  • reporting suspicious transactions and suspicious attempted \r\n transactions, large cash transactions and large EFTs; and
    • \r\n
  • record keeping.
    • \r\n
\r\n

CONTROL POLICIES AND PROCEDURES

\r\n

Control policies and procedures should identify and implement \r\n measures designed to control inherent risks.

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that control policies and procedures are kept \r\n up to date to mitigate risks. They must also comply with other regulatory \r\n requirements: for example, the PCMLTFRFootnote 14 \r\n requires that written compliance policies and procedures form part \r\n of the AML/ATF compliance program and be approved by a senior officer. \r\n

\r\n

Control policies and procedures should be embedded in business \r\n areas commensurate with the risks they are intended to mitigate, \r\n and otherwise tailored to the particular circumstances in which \r\n they operate.

\r\n

Policies

\r\n

AML/ATF policies should set risk management standards to govern \r\n the approach of the FRFI to deterring and detecting ML and TF, and \r\n should ensure regulatory compliance.

\r\n

Policies setting a corporate standard should be approved by the \r\n Board and implemented consistently across the enterprise. They should \r\n establish clear and definitive requirements throughout the organization. \r\n

\r\n

In keeping with the general principle that the corporate standard \r\n should be consistent with Canadian regulatory requirements (see \r\n "Policies" above), policies should implement the corporate standard, \r\n at least, of AML/ATF program requirements in wholly owned subsidiaries \r\n and branches outside Canada to the extent that laws of the foreign \r\n jurisdictions permit it. Policies should also reflect that unless \r\n there is an explicit prohibition, the corporate standard, at least, \r\n should be applied.

\r\n

It should be noted that differences in local market conditions \r\n are not a sound basis for lowering or eliminating enterprise standards. \r\n In such cases, FRFIs should ensure that a specific risk assessment \r\n is made to determine whether operating in such markets would result \r\n in an unacceptable ML or TF risk to the FRFI.

\r\n

Examples of topics that should be covered by policies are:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • what money laundering is. FRFIs should ensure that their policies \r\n and procedures adequately address their exposure to the stages \r\n of money laundering (placement, layering and integration) and \r\n are not unduly limited to anti-placement measures (for example, \r\n prohibitions or restrictions on the acceptance of cash);
    • \r\n
\r\n
    \r\n
  • objectives of the AML/ATF program;
    • \r\n
  • key areas of inherent risk;
    • \r\n
  • Client due diligence standards reflecting: \r\n
      \r\n
    • minimum acceptable client identification requirements, verification \r\n standards, information gathering and monitoring;
      • \r\n
    • prohibition on entering client relationships or processing \r\n transactions if identity cannot be ascertained;
      • \r\n
    • appropriate or prescribed restrictions on entering client \r\n relationships or processing transactions before identity is \r\n established; the types of clients considered higher risk or \r\n not acceptable;
      • \r\n
    • a definition of enhanced due diligence applicable to such \r\n higher risk clients; reporting; and
      • \r\n
    • records retention;
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • dealing with clients who exhibit levels of risk that are unacceptable \r\n to a FRFI;
    • \r\n
  • identification of clients whose accounts were opened prior \r\n to the coming into effect of the 2002 regulatory requirements \r\n and the PCMLTFR, and who have not been identified in accordance \r\n with the PCMLTFR, if such clients or their activities are assessed \r\n as being high riskFootnote 15; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • business rules defining what are unusual transactions and which \r\n unusual transactions are suspicious; and
    • \r\n
  • the mandates of key risk management control functions such as \r\n the Board, Senior Management, the CAMLO, the Auditor, and others. \r\n
    • \r\n
\r\n

Procedures

\r\n

Procedures are the tools FRFIs use to translate AML/ATF policies \r\n into practice. Therefore, it is essential that procedures state \r\n clearly what actions are to be taken, by whom, where and when (noting \r\n pertinent regulatory deadlines as appropriate).

\r\n

The evolving nature of AML/ATF regulation and changes to a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n business require that procedures be updated on a regular basis to \r\n ensure their continued effectiveness. Should a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s procedures \r\n allow for permitted exceptions, the procedures should include authorization \r\n processes and associated enforcement mechanisms to oversee such \r\n exceptions.

\r\n

CLIENT DUE DILIGENCE (CDD)

\r\n

CDD is comprised of client identification, information gathering, \r\n ascertaining identity and ongoing monitoring. These components must \r\n comply with applicable regulatory requirements, and must be enhanced \r\n for higher risk situationsFootnote 16 \r\n . The extent of CDD performed should correspond to the relative \r\n level of assessed ML and TF risks in the circumstances. See \xe2\x80\x9cSpecific \r\n Higher Risks\xe2\x80\x9d below.

\r\n

As a general principle, a business relationship should only be \r\n entered into or maintained with a client if the FRFI is satisfied \r\n that the information it has gathered demonstrates that the FRFI \r\n knows the client (i.e. the client has disclosed his or her true \r\n identity and a legitimate purpose for entering or maintaining the \r\n business relationship with the FRFI). DTIs are required to keep \r\n a record of the intended use of each account opened, other than \r\n a credit card accountFootnote 17 \r\n .

\r\n

The prescribed rules comprising CDD requirements do not permit \r\n FRFIs to establish anonymousFootnote 18 \r\n accounts for clients. If FRFIs provide services (such as account \r\n numbering or coding services) that effectively shield the identity \r\n of a client for business reasons (for instance, in a corporate acquisition \r\n where the premature circulation of information could jeopardize \r\n the transaction), or where client identity is withheld for proprietary \r\n reasons, FRFIs must ensure that they have appropriately ascertained \r\n the identity of the client and that this information is accessible \r\n by the CAMLO.

\r\n

Where the regulatory requirements prescribe a determination of \r\n the status of a client, for example, the determination of whether \r\n a client is a PEFP, there must actually be a determination and FRFIs \r\n should ensure that a determination is made based on an assessment \r\n of the information received.

\r\n

Nature and Amount

\r\n

The nature and extent of CDD measures should be appropriate for \r\n the nature of, and proportional to the level of, the ML and TF risk \r\n that is posed by the client in the circumstances. See \xe2\x80\x9dInherent \r\n Risk Assessment\xe2\x80\x9d, above. At a minimum, CDD measures must comply \r\n with the requirements of the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR. CDD standards \r\n should provide that where there are doubtsFootnote 19 about the veracity or adequacy of previously \r\n obtained client identification and verification data, enhanced CDD \r\n must be performed.

\r\n

FRFIs should enhance CDD measures if standard measures produce \r\n inconsistent, otherwise uncertain or doubtful results. The level \r\n of such enhanced due diligence should be sufficient to mitigate \r\n the inconsistencies, uncertain or doubtful results.

\r\n

Client Identification and Ascertaining of \r\n Identity

\r\n

FRFIs may have clients whose identities have not been ascertained \r\n in accordance with the PCMLTFR on account of having become clients \r\n prior to the AML/ATF requirements coming into force in 2002, or \r\n having purchased products that the PCMLTFR exempt from client identification \r\n requirements. FRFIs should ensure that if such clients subsequently \r\n purchase products to which client identification requirements apply, \r\n they are subject to appropriate client identification measures. \r\n

\r\n

Reasonable measures to ensure that such clients are appropriately \r\n identified could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • ascertaining the identity of the client in respect of each product \r\n purchased; and
    • \r\n
  • establishing systems that flag otherwise unidentified clients \r\n who purchase products subject to prescribed client identification \r\n requirements.
    • \r\n
\r\n

The PCMLTFR specifies the originals of prescribed valid documents \r\n (or types of valid documents) that may be inspected to ascertain \r\n the identity of individuals and the existence of entities in face \r\n to face and non face to face scenarios, and the timing for doing \r\n so. A FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s CCD policy should provide clear direction that complies \r\n with the PCMLTFR, (where applicable) on:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • when a client\xe2\x80\x99s identity must be ascertained (timing);
    • \r\n
  • how to ascertain the identity of the client, when the client \r\n is present or not present; and
    • \r\n
  • which original and valid identification documents should be \r\n used to ascertain identification and what information is to be \r\n recorded from them.
    • \r\n
\r\n

While identification and verification standards and policies must \r\n meet the minimum prescribed requirements, FRFIs may consider that \r\n the assessment of inherent risk justifies the application of additional \r\n identification requirements to some categories of client.

\r\n

For example: the PCMLTFRFootnote 20 prescribes the use of valid government-issued \r\n documents to be used to ascertain the identity of a client. These \r\n include, inter alia, birth certificates. The PCMLTFR permits \r\n Social Insurance Number (SIN) cards to be used to ascertain the \r\n identity of a client. Where a birth certificate or a SIN card is \r\n the only document available to ascertain identity, and the assessed \r\n ML or TF risk of the client is other than minimal, FRFIs should \r\n consider applying additional identification measures. Such additional \r\n measures could include viewing the original of other acceptable \r\n government-issued identification documents, including government-issued \r\n photo identification, or, if these are not available, other credible \r\n evidence supporting the identity of the client such as a property \r\n tax or utility bill.

\r\n

For persons without acceptable Canadian identification documents, \r\n comparable or equivalent foreign identification documents may be \r\n acceptable if they can be read and assessed as valid identification \r\n documents (for example, by reference to publicly available information) \r\n and can be understood by the FRFI.

\r\n

Identifying a client that is a corporation or other entity may \r\n involve the collection of substantial information in some cases. \r\n In addition to confirming the existence of the entityFootnote 21, FRFIs must take reasonable measures \r\n to obtain the names and occupations of its directors and the names, \r\n addresses and occupations of individual(s) who are the ultimate \r\n beneficial owners of 25% or more of the entity Footnote 22. Reasonable \r\n measures to obtain this information could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • requesting it from the entity;
    • \r\n
  • consulting a credible public or other database; or
    • \r\n
  • a combination of both.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Where a FRFI is required to obtain the occupation of a person \r\n (for example, a director of a client entity), the FRFI should ensure \r\n that the occupation obtained is the person\'s principal occupation \r\n and not merely the person\'s title in the client entity.

\r\n

The measures applied should be commensurate with the level of \r\n assessed risk.

\r\n

DTIs must also ascertain the identity of every person who signs \r\n a signature card in respect of a business account, except that where \r\n the signature card is signed by more than three authorized individuals, \r\n the identities of at least three of them must be ascertainedFootnote 23. The requirements of identification \r\n of individual clients are applicable. Life insurance companies should \r\n adopt a similar practice as a matter of prudent risk management \r\n because the inherent risk of not identifying signing officers for \r\n business accounts is similar.

\r\n

The PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR prohibit FRFIs from opening accounts in \r\n prescribed circumstances if the FRFI cannot establish the identity \r\n of the client in accordance with prescribed measuresFootnote 24.

\r\n

FRFIs must also take reasonable measures, at times prescribed \r\n by the PCMLTFRFootnote 25, to determine whether the individual \r\n client is acting for or on behalf of a third party. Reasonable measures \r\n could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • asking the question on a product application; or
    • \r\n
  • including a negative assurance statement above the client\'s \r\n signature line on the application or other purchase document. \r\n
    • \r\n
\r\n

Life Insurance Companies

\r\n

Life insurance companies are not required to ascertain the identity \r\n of, or obtain the identification information of, a person where \r\n there are reasonable grounds to believe that the person\xe2\x80\x99s identity \r\n has been ascertained in the prescribed manner by another life insurance \r\n company or life insurance broker or agent in respect of the same \r\n transaction or of a transaction that is part of a series of transactions \r\n that includes the original transactionFootnote 26. For these situations, life insurance \r\n companies should therefore develop and implement policies and procedures \r\n designed to ensure that:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • they perform appropriate initial and ongoing due diligence \r\n on other life insurance companies, life insurance brokers or agents; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • there are reasonable grounds to believe that the client identification \r\n and verification procedures used by such other life insurance \r\n companies, life insurance brokers or agents comply with the PCMLTFA \r\n and PCMLTFR and with the life insurance company\xe2\x80\x99s own policies \r\n and procedures.
    • \r\n
\r\n

OSFI understands that with respect to individual products, in \r\n practice life insurance companies do receive information about the \r\n identity of the client on application forms submitted by life insurance \r\n agents or brokers. This practice enables life insurance companies \r\n to periodically determine that the grounds for relying on such agents \r\n are reasonable.

\r\n

Source of Accumulated Funds or Wealth

\r\n

FRFIs should satisfy themselves that, in appropriate circumstances, \r\n the amount of clients\xe2\x80\x99 accumulated funds or wealth appears to be \r\n reasonable and consistent with the information provided. Doubts \r\n about the origin of such funds or wealth should be satisfied before \r\n proceeding with the relationship or permitting transactions to occur. \r\n Reasonable measures to implement this requirement could include: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • obtaining and evaluating more detailed information from the \r\n client; and
    • \r\n
  • verifying information obtained from other financial institutions \r\n or references.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Where doubts persist, consideration should be given to not proceeding \r\n with the relationship or transaction.

\r\n

In cases where a client is assessed as higher risk and \r\n the source of accumulated funds or wealth does not appear \r\n to be reasonable, or is inconsistent with the information provided \r\n despite taking reasonable measures to resolve the inconsistency, \r\n the FRFI should consider declining to enter the business relationship, \r\n or terminating it, and consider filing a suspicious attempted transaction \r\n report.

\r\n

Monitoring

\r\n

Standard

\r\n

FRFIs must be able to identify suspicious transactions, or suspicious \r\n attempted transactions, and report these to FINTRAC. Further, FRFIs \r\n must take reasonable measures to ascertain the identity of every \r\n person with whom the FRFI conducts a transaction that is determined \r\n by the FRFI to be suspiciousFootnote 27. \r\n These obligations imply that the activities of all clients, regardless \r\n of their risk ranking, must be subject to some form of ongoing monitoring \r\n to detect transactions or attempted transactions that are potentially \r\n suspicious.

\r\n

Reasonable measures for such monitoring could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Identification and review of types of transactions or attempted \r\n transactions (defined by size, frequency, geographical location, \r\n delivery channel, business relationship or other factors) that \r\n appear to be inconsistent with the intended purpose of the account \r\n or the circumstances; and
    • \r\n
  • Changes in transaction activity that may on their own or in \r\n conjunction with recorded changes in client information, be indicative \r\n of a change in the nature of a client\'s business or intended use \r\n of the account.
    • \r\n
\r\n

FRFIs should conduct feasibility studies, as appropriate, to determine \r\n whether transaction volumes merit the application of information \r\n technology solutions to transaction monitoring.

\r\n

Monitoring should identify information, transactions or attempted \r\n transactions that are unusual or potentially suspicious and that \r\n require further analysis. Monitoring criteria should cover all relevant \r\n indicators. Relevant indicators could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • frequent and unexplained movement of accounts to different \r\n financial institutions;
    • \r\n
  • frequent and unexplained movement of funds between different \r\n financial institutions in various geographic locations;
    • \r\n
  • client information about or explanations for the source of \r\n transaction funds or accumulated wealth that is not clearly reasonable \r\n or credible;
    • \r\n
  • transactions that are structured or otherwise complex, or unusually \r\n large relative to the size and business of the client or the geographical \r\n location of the transaction;
    • \r\n
  • types of transactions, or patterns of transactions, inconsistent \r\n with the purpose of the account or the business of the client; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • transactions that have no apparent economic or visible lawful \r\n purpose.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Enhanced

\r\n

The PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR provide that where a FRFI determines that \r\n the risk of a ML or TF offence is high, FRFIs must take prescribed \r\n special measures for identifying clients, keeping records and monitoring \r\n financial transactions in respect of the activities that pose the \r\n high riskFootnote 28. The prescribed \r\n special measures include: reasonable measures to determine whether \r\n the high risk client is a PEFPFootnote 29; \r\n keep client identification information and the information referred \r\n to in PCMLTFR s. 11.1 up to dateFootnote 30; \r\n conduct ongoing suspicious transaction and suspicious attempted \r\n transaction monitoring Footnote 31; \r\n and generally mitigate the high riskFootnote 32. \r\n

\r\n

FRFIs should consider creating more than one category of higher \r\n risk client, and more than one category of enhanced due diligence, \r\n if the nature, scope, complexity and risk profile of the financial \r\n institution merit such action. Each level of enhanced monitoring \r\n should reflect the assessed level of risk appropriately.

\r\n

Reasonable measures for applying enhanced monitoring could include: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • More frequent reviews of client activity and types of activity; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • More frequent updates or reviews of client information;
    • \r\n
  • The application of additional client identification measures; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • The gathering of information from public or open sources such \r\n as commercial databases;
    • \r\n
  • More frequent flagging of unusual transactions or other information; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • Referral of client activity and transactions to a more senior \r\n officer in the FRFI for review.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Additional measures that could be taken to strengthen the monitoring \r\n of high risk activities include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Review of business reports, including exceptions reports, generated \r\n by management information systems (for example, anti-fraud systems), \r\n for possible indicators in them of unusual or suspicious activity. \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Analysis of STR information for trends and other indicators \r\n of suspicious activity to aid the development of appropriate risk-based \r\n controls in businesses that indicate such activity.
    • \r\n
\r\n

SPECIFIC HIGHER RISKS

\r\n

This section discusses OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s expectations and prescribed measures \r\n in respect of enhanced due diligence and related controls applicable \r\n to areas of identified higher risk.

\r\n

Use of Agents or Mandataries

\r\n

Many FRFIs rely on introducers, intermediaries or other third \r\n partiesFootnote 33 \r\n for client information gathering and verification purposes. These \r\n include, for example, deposit and mortgage brokers and solicitors. \r\n ML and TF risk mitigation can be compromised where FRFIs do not \r\n ensure that appropriate client identification standards are applied \r\n by the introducers, intermediaries or other third parties.

\r\n

With one exception for life insurance companies referred to above, \r\n accountability for ascertaining the identity of the client and obtaining \r\n the information used to identify the client remains with the FRFI \r\n when it uses a third party to ascertain the identity of clients. \r\n In respect of this accountability, FRFIs must have an agreement \r\n or arrangement in writing with the agent or mandatary if such person \r\n is to be responsible for client identification and verification. \r\n The provisions of this arrangement or agreement must conform to \r\n the requirements of the PCMLTFRFootnote 34 \r\n and it should obligate the agent or mandatary to:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • apply the DTI\xe2\x80\x99s or life insurance company\xe2\x80\x99s client identification \r\n and verification requirements (which must comply with the regulatory \r\n requirements);
    • \r\n
  • ensure that, where the client is present at the time client \r\n identification is ascertained, the agent or mandatary applies \r\n client identification procedures that include viewing original \r\n identification documents;
    • \r\n
  • ensure that, where the client is not present at the time client \r\n identification is ascertained, the agent or mandatary applies \r\n prescribed non-face-to-face identification requirements Footnote 35; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • provide the client identification information to the DTI promptly \r\n after obtaining it.
    • \r\n
\r\n

DTIs and life insurance companies should also:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • ensure that if the agent or mandatary is responsible for collecting \r\n the information required to make a third party determination or \r\n a PEFP determination, these responsibilities are also documented; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Ensure they receive client identification information in the \r\n required timeframes; and
    • \r\n
  • periodically review, in a systemic manner, the quality of client \r\n information gathered and documented by the agent or mandatary \r\n to ensure that it continues to meet their requirements.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Documentation of relationships and communications with, and client \r\n due diligence work of, agents and mandataries, should be complete \r\n and current, and client information should be placed in the client\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n record promptly upon receiving it. See further, \xe2\x80\x9cRecord Keeping \r\n and Retention\xe2\x80\x9d, below.

\r\n

FRFIs should consider terminating relationships with agents or \r\n mandataries that do not comply with agreed upon client identification \r\n responsibilities or provide the DTI or life insurance company with \r\n the requisite client information on a timely basis.

\r\n

Contracts with agents and mandataries should be reviewed and updated \r\n as necessary to ensure compliance with the PCMLTFRFootnote 36 regarding the use of agents and mandataries. \r\n

\r\n

The extent of the DTI\xe2\x80\x99s or life insurance company\xe2\x80\x99s exposure to \r\n the agent or mandatary for the results of client due diligence should \r\n be addressed expressly in the DTI\xe2\x80\x99s or life insurance company\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n inherent risk assessment.

\r\n

Fraud with respect to Mortgage Loans and \r\n other Products

\r\n

Fraudulent misrepresentation in respect of FRFIs\' products takes \r\n many forms that could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Forged or falsified employment letters or references, or misrepresented \r\n self-employment;
    • \r\n
  • Forged or falsified pay stubs, T4 slips, and CRA Notices of \r\n Assessment;
    • \r\n
  • Forged or falsified personal identification documents;
    • \r\n
  • Use of "straw" (i.e., non-existent) individuals;
    • \r\n
  • False or falsified credit records;
    • \r\n
  • Concealed legal or beneficial ownership;
    • \r\n
  • Concealed sources of down payment; and
    • \r\n
  • Inflated assets.
    • \r\n
\r\n

FRFIs should ensure their client acceptance and due diligence \r\n processes address the risk of fraud, a predicate offence for money \r\n laundering. FRFIs should take reasonable measures to address the \r\n risk, which could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Applying enhanced client identification measures such as viewing \r\n a second piece of identification, or viewing government-issued \r\n photo identification;
    • \r\n
  • Having an agent or mandatary apply enhanced non-face-to-face \r\n client identification measures;
    • \r\n
  • Ensuring that legal and/or beneficial ownership of property \r\n or business is understood and documented;
    • \r\n
  • Satisfying themselves that the amount of clients\' accumulated \r\n funds or wealth appears to be reasonable and consistent with the \r\n information provided (see further, "Source of Accumulated Funds \r\n or Wealth" above);
    • \r\n
  • Training staff, agents or mandataries in the recognition of \r\n valid identification documents and signs of falsification of documents; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Obtaining corroboration of information in employer letters, \r\n references, pay stubs or credit records, as appropriate; and
    • \r\n
  • Corroborating the existence and value of stated assets.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Life insurance companies should ensure that mortgage loans are \r\n subject to the AML/ATF program.

\r\n

PEFPs

\r\n

The FATF Recommendations state that PEPs are potentially more \r\n susceptible to financial crime than other clients of financial institutions. \r\n In Canada, the PCMLTFA requires FRFIs to determine, in prescribed \r\n circumstances, whether they are dealing with PEFPs and also prescribes \r\n mandatory enhanced due diligence measures to be taken in respect \r\n of PEFPs in prescribed circumstances.Footnote 37 \r\n

\r\n

A PEFP is defined in the PCMLTFA as an individual who holds or \r\n has ever held prescribed offices or positions in or on behalf of \r\n a foreign state or is a prescribed member of the family of such \r\n a personFootnote 38. \r\n

\r\n

For purposes of the foregoing, the term "foreign state" should \r\n be interpreted to include the principal political subdivisions of \r\n foreign countries when applying the PEFP definition.

\r\n

Once the determination is made, prescribed actions must be taken \r\n within minimum time periods.

\r\n

Timing of PEFP determination - DTIs

\r\n

There are three situations that trigger the requirement for DTIs \r\n to determine whether a client is a PEFP:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • when an account is openedFootnote 39; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • when an existing client is deemed to be high riskFootnote 40; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • when a client initiates or receives an EFT of $100,000 or more \r\n Footnote 41. \r\n
    • \r\n
\r\n

The determination and approval by a senior officer to keep the \r\n account open must be made no later than 14 days from account activation \r\n Footnote 42 or within \r\n 14 days of the EFT being received or sentFootnote 43. \r\n There is no specific time period in respect of determination as \r\n a result of a risk assessment. FRFIs should ensure that the PEFP \r\n determination required when an existing account is deemed to be \r\n high risk is made no later than 14 days thereafter, to be consistent \r\n with other prescribed requirements.

\r\n

Timing of PEFP determination \xe2\x80\x93 Life Insurance Companies

\r\n

Life insurance companies must take reasonable measures to determine \r\n if a person who makes a lump-sum payment of $100,000 or more in \r\n relation to an immediate or deferred annuity or life insurance policy \r\n on their own behalf or on behalf of a third party is a PEFP Footnote 44. \r\n Such person may not be the policy holder.

\r\n

The determination must be made within 14 days of the payment transaction \r\n Footnote 45. \r\n

\r\n

Points to Consider in Making a PEFP Determination

\r\n

The PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR require that FRFIs take \xe2\x80\x9creasonable measures\xe2\x80\x9d \r\n to make the PEFP determination. Reasonable measures could include:
\r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Asking the individual for information that could indicate PEFP \r\n status, such as existing or previous connections to the prescribed \r\n relationships;
    • \r\n
  • Screening the individual\xe2\x80\x99s name and other personal information \r\n against a commercially or publicly available database to gather \r\n more information about the individual; or
    • \r\n
  • a combination of both.
    • \r\n
\r\n

About asking the Client

\r\n

If FRFIs choose to ask the individual for information, FRFIs should \r\n keep in mind that clients should not be expected to know the criteria \r\n that determine whether they are PEFPs. FRFIs should also note that \r\n there is no obligation imposed on FRFIs to disclose to a client \r\n that a determination must be made, or needs to be made.

\r\n

A reasonable approach would be to ask the client if the client \r\n has or has ever had a prescribed connection to a foreign state, \r\n government, military or judiciary. The questions could be expanded \r\n to cover family members with any similar connections. If the responses \r\n are not clear or inconclusive, additional assessment or due diligence \r\n may be necessary before finalizing the determination. The additional \r\n measures could range from asking the applicant for more information, \r\n to internet searches, to running the individual(s\xe2\x80\x99) name(s) against \r\n a public database.

\r\n

FINTRAC has published a pamphlet that FRFIs can use to explain \r\n to their clients, if necessary, why they need to enquire about their \r\n background. This pamphlet can be viewed at FINTRAC\xe2\x80\x99s Internet site. \r\n

\r\n

About consulting a commercial database

\r\n

FRFIs that choose to screen names and other personal information \r\n against a commercial or publicly available database should ensure \r\n they:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Determine whether the provider identifies in the database individuals \r\n who fit the definition of PEFPs in the PCMLFTA and PCMLTFR. Most \r\n of these databases are built using open source (i.e. public) information. \r\n If the family members of a PEFP are not well known, there is no \r\n guarantee that a database will know about them.
    • \r\n
  • Establish the frequency and methodology used to update the \r\n information in the database, including whether the provider removes \r\n names from the database when officeholders leave office or die. \r\n If names are removed, the database may not capture persons who \r\n \xe2\x80\x9chave ever been\xe2\x80\x9d PEFPs.
    • \r\n
  • Establish a process to discard false positive hits, and identify \r\n other steps to be taken if the information in the database is \r\n inconclusive.
    • \r\n
  • Are able to screen the names of clients in all business lines \r\n against this list, especially if the FRFI has manual procedures, \r\n legacy systems, or uses the database to screen for the names of \r\n designated persons under anti-terrorist regulations.
    • \r\n
\r\n

OSFI does not expect FRFIs to depend on a client database in making \r\n a PEFP determination where the information obtained from the client \r\n shows that the client is a PEFP. Clients, who initially provide \r\n information that clearly establishes them to be PEFPs, must be determined \r\n to be PEFPs and need not be scrubbed through databases unless it \r\n is done merely to obtain background or additional information.

\r\n

Refer to the discussion about \xe2\x80\x9creasonable measures\xe2\x80\x9d in "Client \r\n Due Diligence", above. FRFIs should ensure a determination is made \r\n based on an evaluation of the information received from a client \r\n or a database.

\r\n

FRFIs should also ensure that, where a client is determined to \r\n be a PEFP, and the FRFI is aware that the client has family members \r\n who are also PEFPs by reason of the definition in the PCMLTFA, the \r\n names of such family members are scrubbed against the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s client \r\n databases to determine if accounts are held in such names by the \r\n FRFI.

\r\n

FRFIs that use agents or mandataries (deposit brokers, mortgage \r\n brokers or others) to identify their clients and remit client identification \r\n information to them retain responsibility for PEFP determination. \r\n FRFIs may assign responsibility for collecting the information necessary \r\n for the FRFI to determine if the client is a PEFP, but the FRFI, \r\n not the agent, is responsible for making the determination and for \r\n applying the prescribed measures accordingly. FRFIs should ensure \r\n that where agents or mandataries are responsible for gathering the \r\n information, the agents understand what is required to be done and \r\n the FRFI satisfies itself that its agents are doing what is required. \r\n

\r\n

If a client\xe2\x80\x99s name is contained in a public database, but the \r\n FRFI does not determine the client is a PEFP, the FRFI may wish \r\n to make a note of the \xe2\x80\x9chit\xe2\x80\x9d for future reference or to guide it \r\n in any future risk assessment.

\r\n

What Happens after a PEFP Determination is made

\r\n

Once a PEFP determination is made, it may not be reversed or otherwise \r\n changed, other than to correct error. The PEFP definition provides \r\n that the criterion or criteria that trigger PEFP status remain(s) \r\n in effect in perpetuity.

\r\n

When a client is determined to be a PEFP a FRFI must:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Take reasonable measures to establish the source (i.e., how \r\n the client acquired the funds in the account) of the PEFP\xe2\x80\x99s funds; \r\n see \xe2\x80\x9cApplying PEFP Determination to Canadian sources of funds \r\n or payments\xe2\x80\x9d, below;
    • \r\n
  • For DTIs, obtain the approval of a senior officer to keep the \r\n account open; for life insurance companies, ensure that a senior \r\n officer reviews the transaction; and
    • \r\n
  • Conduct enhanced ongoing monitoring of the PEFP\xe2\x80\x99s account to \r\n identify potentially suspicious transactions.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Reasonable measures to establish source(s) of funds include asking \r\n the client to explain how the client came to hold the funds. Examples \r\n of source of funds could include: savings accumulated through employment; \r\n sale of investments; sale of a business; an inheritance; a salary \r\n bonus; and consulting fees.

\r\n

In respect of the approval by a senior officer, such individual \r\n should be a person at a more senior level who has the authority \r\n to make this decision.

\r\n

Reasonable measures for enhanced and ongoing monitoring of PEFPs\xe2\x80\x99 \r\n accounts may involve manual or automated processes, or a combination \r\n of both depending on resources and needs and could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Developing reports or performing more frequent review of PEFP \r\n account activity, and flagging activities that deviate from expectations \r\n and elevate concerns as necessary;
    • \r\n
  • Setting up a management committee to regularly review all identified \r\n PEFPs and their transactions; and
    • \r\n
  • Reviewing transactions more frequently against indicators of \r\n suspicious transactions.
    • \r\n
\r\n

PEFPs in Canada and Domestic PEPs

\r\n

The PEFP definition in the PCMLTFA indicates that the country \r\n of residence or citizenship of an individual is immaterial to PEFP \r\n determination. FRFIs should therefore ensure that their methodology \r\n of PEFP determination does not preclude individuals merely because \r\n they may be Canadian citizens or residents.

\r\n

FRFIs may need to ensure they distinguish between PEFPs and domestic \r\n PEPs. The latter are not separately defined in the PCMLTFA definition \r\n of PEFP, although a PEFP could also be a domestic PEP. However, \r\n FRFIs are not under any legal obligation to identify domestic PEPs \r\n per se, whether by screening or flagging large transactions \r\n or in any other way. Further, even if FRFIs know they are dealing \r\n with a domestic PEP, they are not under any legal obligation to \r\n apply the measures that are applicable to PEFP accounts, unless \r\n that individual is a PEFP.

\r\n

Where a FRFI is aware that a client is a domestic PEP, the FRFI \r\n should assess what effect, if any, this may have on the overall \r\n assessed risk of the client. If the assessed risk is elevated, the \r\n FRFI should apply enhanced due diligence measures as it considers \r\n appropriate.

\r\n

Identification of PEFPs in Foreign Subsidiaries or Branches

\r\n

The PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR do not oblige FRFIs to apply PEFP measures \r\n to their subsidiaries or branches of FRFIs outside Canada.

\r\n

Where a FRFI is aware that a client of a subsidiary or a branch \r\n outside Canada is a PEFP, the FRFI should assess what effect, if \r\n any, this may have on the overall assessed risk of the client. If \r\n the assessed risk is elevated, the FRFI should apply enhanced due \r\n diligence as it considers appropriate.

\r\n

The operations of foreign branches and subsidiaries may be subject \r\n to local AML/ATF legislation, which may include requirements to \r\n identify and monitor PEPs, including PEFPs.

\r\n

Identification of PEFPs who own or control 25% or more of Clients \r\n that are Corporations or Entities, or who are directors or officers \r\n of such corporations or entities

\r\n

FRFIs are not obliged by the PCMLTFA or PCMLTFR to apply PEFP \r\n determination procedures to persons who own or control 25% or more \r\n of clients that are corporations or entities, or who are directors \r\n or officers of such corporations or entities.

\r\n

Where a FRFI is aware that a person who owns or controls 25% or \r\n more of a client that is a corporation or entity, or who is a director \r\n or officer of such a corporation or entity, is a PEFP, the FRFI \r\n should assess what effect, if any, this may have on the overall \r\n assessed risk of the client corporation or entity. If the assessed \r\n risk is elevated, the FRFI should apply enhanced due diligence as \r\n it considers appropriate. Appropriate due diligence could include: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • A determination as to whether the PEFP is a client of the FRFI, \r\n and, if so, whether enhanced monitoring procedures should apply \r\n to the client\xe2\x80\x99s and the PEFP\xe2\x80\x99s transactions.
    • \r\n
  • Enhanced monitoring of the client account.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Applying PEFP determinations to Canadian sources of funds or \r\n payments

\r\n

For life insurance companies, the PCMLTFRFootnote 46 does not distinguish between domestic \r\n and foreign payments. Accordingly, life insurance companies should \r\n apply a PEFP determination to prescribed funds from any source, \r\n domestic or foreign.

\r\n

For DTIs, domestic transfers into or out of an account \r\n do not, of themselves, trigger any requirement to make a PEFP determination \r\n Footnote 47. \r\n

\r\n

However, if a DTI has already determined that a client is a PEFP, \r\n OSFI believes that a risk assessment should be made to determine \r\n whether monitoring domestic incoming transfers would be advisable. \r\n

\r\n

Client Corporations that can issue bearer \r\n shares

\r\n

Identifying a client that is a corporation that can issue bearer \r\n shares may require special customer identification measures. Bearer \r\n shares can hide the identity of beneficial owners of the client \r\n corporation. If the aggregate of such shares could amount to more \r\n than 25% of such client corporation, a FRFI might be unable to identify \r\n the beneficial owner(s).

\r\n

Where a FRFI assesses (using the risk categories outline above) \r\n that the risk of dealing with such a client corporation may be present, \r\n the FRFI should apply reasonable measures to mitigate this risk. \r\n Reasonable measures should always include obtaining the identity \r\n of the person or persons who beneficially own 25% or more of the \r\n shares of the corporation taking into account any issued and outstanding \r\n bearer shares, and could also include one or more of the following: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Requesting the client corporation to immobilize any issued \r\n and outstanding bearer shares, for example, by arranging for the \r\n certificates representing such shares to be placed with a custodian \r\n such as a trustee. The arrangement should permit the FRFI to: \r\n
      \r\n
    • Verify on request that the shares continue to be held by \r\n the custodian; and
      • \r\n
    • Be advised on a timely basis of any change in ownership \r\n of the shares that may change this information.
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Requesting the client corporation to amend its charter documents \r\n to remove the power to issue bearer shares and limit the issue \r\n of new shares;
    • \r\n
  • Requesting the client corporation to cancel any issued and \r\n outstanding bearer shares and replace them with shares in registered \r\n form.
    • \r\n
\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that the measures taken are documented.

\r\n

Correspondent Banking

\r\n

For the purpose of this Guideline, "correspondent banking relationship" \r\n has the same meaning as in the PCMLTFA.

\r\n

Correspondent banking relationships are established between banks \r\n to facilitate, among other things, transactions between banks made \r\n on their own behalf; transactions on behalf of their clients; and \r\n making services available directly to clients of other banks. Examples \r\n of these services include: inter-bank deposit activities; international \r\n electronic funds transfers; cash management; cheque clearing and \r\n payment services; collections; payment for foreign exchange services; \r\n processing client payments (in either domestic or foreign currency); \r\n and payable- through accounts.

\r\n

Correspondent banking relationships with foreign financial institutions \r\n (FFIs) are identified by the FATF as a specific higher risk area, \r\n and consequently the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR prescribe measures Footnote 48 to be applied \r\n by FRFIs that enter into correspondent banking relationships with \r\n FFIs and their clients.

\r\n

FRFIs that offer payable through accounts services to customers \r\n of FFIs must take reasonable measures to ascertain whether the FFIs \r\n have met requirements that oblige them to identify and ascertain \r\n the identities of such clients that are consistent with the requirements \r\n of the PCMLTFR, and ensure that the FFIs will provide relevant customer \r\n identification data to the FRFI, upon requestFootnote 49. \r\n Reasonable measures to achieve these requirements could include: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Obtaining copies of the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s AML policies, and in particular \r\n its customer acceptance policies, and reviewing these for consistency \r\n with the requirements of the PCMLTFR;
    • \r\n
  • Ensuring that the documentation of the agreement with the FFI \r\n includes an obligation on the part of the FFI to provide relevant \r\n customer identification information to the FRFI when requested \r\n to do so.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Reasonable measures to monitor correspondent banking relationships \r\n generally could include, for example:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Establish and periodically update an AML country risk rating \r\n system and assign a rating to each country in which a correspondent \r\n banking relationship has been established, for the purpose of \r\n implementing an appropriate level of monitoring;
    • \r\n
  • Review the FATF (or FATF style regional body\xe2\x80\x99s) mutual evaluation \r\n report or other assessment of the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s home country\xe2\x80\x99s measures \r\n to implement the FATF 40 Recommendations and 9 Special Recommendations; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Review the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s ownership and background;
    • \r\n
  • Be satisfied that its activities are authorized, regulated \r\n and supervised by the relevant regulatory authority in its home \r\n country;
    • \r\n
  • Meet or otherwise communicate with senior representatives of \r\n the FFI to understand their commitment to effective control of \r\n ML and TF and understand key provisions of the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s AML/ATF policies \r\n and procedures such as those dealing with client acceptance; and \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Use the services of credible third parties (such as those providing \r\n a document repository or AML/ATF rankings) as a source of additional \r\n information on the FFI and its regulatory environment.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Where a FRFI ascertains, pursuant to s. 55.1 of the PCMLTFR, that \r\n there are civil or criminal sanctions imposed against a FFI in respect \r\n of AML/ATF requirements; or where a FRFI ascertains that a FFI does \r\n not have in place AML/ATF policies and procedures as specified in \r\n ss.15.1(3) of the PCMLTFA; then for the purpose of detecting any \r\n suspicious transactions required to be reported to FINTRAC under \r\n s. 7 of the PCMLTFA the FRFI should conduct ongoing monitoring of \r\n all transactions in the context of the correspondent banking relationship \r\n to mitigate the higher risk Footnote 50. The extent \r\n of such monitoring in the case of sanctions identified against a \r\n FFI should correspond to the context, severity and type of sanctions \r\n imposed on the FFI. Reasonable measures could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Reviewing in more depth the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s client acceptance process \r\n and its process for risk assessing its clients, products and services; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Training officers of the FRFI on the required enhanced transaction \r\n monitoring requirements to be applied with respect to the relationship, \r\n including those transactions of the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s clients that are permitted \r\n to access the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s banking services;
    • \r\n
  • Escalating the level of Senior Management responsible for the \r\n relationship;
    • \r\n
  • Reviewing transactions over threshold amounts (using a risk-based \r\n approach), identified by analyzing client risk, business relationship \r\n risk, product/service risk, delivery channel risk, geographic \r\n risk and other relevant risk factors;
    • \r\n
  • Reviewing the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s methodology for monitoring and surveillance \r\n of transactions, in particular those that ultimately result in \r\n a transaction being processed by the DTI (e.g. an international \r\n wire payment, payment under a letter of credit, etc.) and preparing \r\n a summary of the key AML/ATF policies and procedures of the FFI \r\n as well as providing details on the due diligence carried out; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • Conducting risk-based retrospective due diligence on existing \r\n clients utilizing the correspondent banking relationship using \r\n the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s standards and criteria established in accordance with \r\n the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR; and
    • \r\n
  • Giving consideration to restricting or discontinuing payable-through \r\n account services if the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of the relationship concludes \r\n that the FFI\xe2\x80\x99s polices and procedures do not meet the standards \r\n set out in s. 55.2 of the PCMLTFR.
    • \r\n
\r\n

A FRFI acting as an intermediary bank may not be in a position \r\n to understand the purpose of EFTs originated by clients of FFIs \r\n or other originator banks, or conduct CDD on these persons. Consequently, \r\n such a FRFI that receives a cover payment for transactions may not \r\n be in a position to determine whether EFTs represented by the cover \r\n payment are suspicious, based on an understanding of the activities \r\n of the originator (and the beneficiary, if the beneficiary is not \r\n a client of the FRFI). It is, however, possible for intermediary \r\n FRFIs to monitor transactions that they process to identify patterns \r\n of activity that may be suspicious, to report suspicious transactions \r\n or attempted transactions, and, where such transactions are associated \r\n with a particular FFI, to review the relationship with that FFI. \r\n

\r\n

Processing Electronic Funds Transfers

\r\n

All information prescribed by the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR, including \r\n originator informationFootnote 51, \r\n must be included on all outgoing international EFTs and domestic \r\n SWIFT payments originated by FRFIs.

\r\n

In addition, FRFIs must take reasonable measures to ensure that \r\n incoming EFTs include originator information. FRFIs that act as \r\n intermediary banks should develop and implement reasonable policies \r\n and procedures for monitoring payment message data subsequent to \r\n processing. Such measures should facilitate the detection of instances \r\n where required message fields are completed but the information \r\n is unclear, or where there is meaningless data in message fields. \r\n Reasonable measures could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Contacting the originator\xe2\x80\x99s bank or precedent intermediary \r\n bank to clarify or complete the information received in the required \r\n fields;
    • \r\n
  • considering (in the case of repeated incidents involving the \r\n same correspondent or in cases where a correspondent declines \r\n to provide additional information) whether the relationship with \r\n the correspondent or the intermediary bank should be restricted \r\n or terminated; and/or
    • \r\n
  • filing a suspicious transaction report.
    • \r\n
\r\n

The reasons for decisions taken should be documented.

\r\n

Trade Finance

\r\n

Traditional trade finance services include letters of credit or \r\n other financial products, which give FRFIs the opportunity to view \r\n and assess details of the transaction that triggers an international \r\n payment.

\r\n

FRFIs that outsource trade finance services to other financial \r\n institutions should ensure that this outsourcing is included in \r\n the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s inherent risk assessment. If the assessment indicates \r\n that the risk of ML and TF is elevated, the FRFI should implement \r\n reasonable measures to control the risk. Reasonable measures could \r\n include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Conducting an analysis of the provider\xe2\x80\x99s policies and practices; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • Communicating to the provider what AML/ATF control measures \r\n the FRFI expects the provider to have in place. The FRFI should \r\n have the right to audit such measures.
    • \r\n
\r\n

OSFI recognizes that FRFIs whose services are used to make trade \r\n finance payments on an open account basis may not have an opportunity \r\n to review the nature of a client\'s underlying trade transaction. \r\n Reasonable measures to address this risk could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Periodic verification, using credible open source material \r\n or information, of the business of the client that triggers the \r\n need for such payments;
    • \r\n
  • Periodic review of electronic funds transfer data to determine \r\n whether the client\'s business includes significant trade activity; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • Periodic review of the client\'s transactions compared to the \r\n FRFI\'s record of the intended purpose of the account;
    • \r\n
  • Meeting or other interaction with the client; or
    • \r\n
  • Periodic confirmation that the client is not in a type of business \r\n to which the FRFI has decided, as a matter of policy, not to provide \r\n financial services.
    • \r\n
\r\n

Under- and over-Invoicing of Goods and Services

\r\n

The FATF has advisedFootnote 52 \r\n that the laundering of funds through under- and over-invoicing is \r\n one of the oldest methods of fraudulently transferring value across \r\n borders, and remains a common practice. The key element of the technique \r\n is the misrepresentation of the price of the good or service in \r\n order to transfer additional value between the importer and exporter. \r\n Many such cases have been identified by the FATF.

\r\n

Multiple Invoicing of Goods and Services

\r\n

By invoicing the same good or service more than once, a money \r\n launderer may be able to justify multiple payments for the same \r\n shipment of goods or delivery of services, especially if more than \r\n one financial institution is used. Multiple invoicing avoids the \r\n need to misrepresent prices.

\r\n

Over- and Under-Shipments of Goods and Services

\r\n

A third method of illicitly moving funds is to move more, less, \r\n or no goods.

\r\n

Other and More Complex Trade-based Money Laundering Techniques \r\n

\r\n

The foregoing techniques can be combined in more complex series \r\n of arrangements. For example, the so-called Black Market Peso Exchange \r\n is a known technique used to launder the proceeds of the sale of \r\n drugs. For more detailed information on techniques and typologies \r\n associated with this and other trade-based money laundering, FRFIs \r\n are requested to consult FATF material available on the FATF web \r\n site.

\r\n

Assessing the Risks in Trade Finance Services, and Enhanced Measures \r\n to Mitigate Assessed Risk

\r\n

Where the assessed risk of ML or TF in trade finance services \r\n is elevated, FRFIs should take reasonable measures designed to mitigate \r\n the risk of misuse of trade financing mechanisms. Reasonable measures \r\n could include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Conducting periodic on-site assessment of the risks posed by \r\n clients and the procedures they follow;
    • \r\n
  • Reviewing the routing of shipments and note ports of call or \r\n transhipment points that are inconsistent with a standard commercial \r\n transaction, for example, a shipment of steel from Canada to Asia \r\n routed via a European port or a country where there is no apparent \r\n business rationale for the routing, or where the routing or the \r\n carrier is located in a high risk country;
    • \r\n
  • Subjecting requests involving letters of credit to cover shipments \r\n of goods that are not consistent with the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s normal business \r\n patterns to more detailed review and noting the results in the \r\n client\xe2\x80\x99s records;
    • \r\n
  • Identifying significant differences (either between different \r\n clients, different shipments or market quotes) in prices of a \r\n good or commodity being financed under a letter of credit, and \r\n determining the business rationale for the differences; and
    • \r\n
  • Making additional enquiries about the business rationale of \r\n transactions involving multiple banks and payments flowing through \r\n intermediaries as opposed to directly from the importer\xe2\x80\x99s bank \r\n to the exporter\xe2\x80\x99s bank.
    • \r\n
\r\n

New and Developing Technologies

\r\n

Developments in technology frequently drive the creation of new \r\n financial products and services. Such developments can lower costs, \r\n improve client service and expand markets. FRFIs should have policies \r\n and procedures in place to ensure that new and developing technologies \r\n are included in the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s inherent risk assessment process. In \r\n this way FRFIs can ensure that appropriate AML/ATF controls are \r\n in place, and, where appropriate, develop or amend controls to take \r\n new risks into account. Examples of new and developing technologies \r\n include stored value cards that may permit clients to subsequently \r\n download those funds directly into a deposit or a credit account \r\n and mobile telephone technology and various e-money services that \r\n have similar characteristics.

RECORD KEEPING AND RETENTION

\r\n

Procedures for keeping paper and electronic records of pertinent \r\n information about clients and transactions must ensure that the \r\n FRFI complies with all of the record keeping requirements of the \r\n PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR. These include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • for clients that are entities: prescribed information, if obtained, \r\n about beneficial owners of corporate clients and other prescribed \r\n information on corporations and other entitiesFootnote 53; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for large cash transactions: large cash transaction records \r\n Footnote 54; \r\n related client recordsFootnote 55; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for account opening: prescribed information about client individuals \r\n and entities for non- credit card account openingFootnote 56; \r\n and prescribed information about credit card holders for credit \r\n card account openingFootnote 57; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for account operation: account operating agreements Footnote 58and \r\n other prescribed informationFootnote 59 \r\n for non-credit card accounts; and credit card account records \r\n for credit card accounts;Footnote 60; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for credit transactions: new credit filesFootnote 61; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for currency exchange: foreign currency exchange transaction \r\n ticketsFootnote 62; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for transactions of $3,000 and more with non-account holders: \r\n prescribed information for traveller\xe2\x80\x99s cheques, money orders or \r\n similar negotiable instrumentsFootnote 63 \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for prescribed incoming EFTs: prescribed informationFootnote 64; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for trusts with respect to which trust companies are trustees: \r\n copy of trust deed and other prescribed informationFootnote 65; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for accounts of PEFPs: PEFP office or position and other prescribed \r\n informationFootnote 66; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for transactions of PEFPs: PEFP office or position and other \r\n prescribed information Footnote 67; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for credit card accounts, account opening and accounts of PEFPs: \r\n PEFP office or position and other prescribed information Footnote 68; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for foreign correspondent banking relationships: name and address \r\n and other prescribed informationFootnote 69; \r\n
    • \r\n
  • for purchases from life insurance companies of immediate or \r\n deferred annuities or life insurance policies for which the client \r\n may pay $10,000 or more over the duration of the annuity or policy: \r\n client information recordFootnote 70; \r\n and
    • \r\n
  • for suspicious transactions and suspicious attempted transactions: \r\n investigations and conclusions.
    • \r\n
\r\n

FRFIs are expected to use record keeping methodologies and formats \r\n that are appropriate in their particular circumstances, provided \r\n that records required to be kept by the PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR must, \r\n as a general rule, be kept for at least 5 yearsFootnote 71 \r\n and they must be made available to competent authorities on a timely \r\n basis, which is within 30 days after a request is made Footnote 72. \r\n

\r\n

Client information should be kept current to reflect regulatory \r\n requirements and the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s continuing knowledge of the client, \r\n client activities and purpose of the client relationship, which \r\n facilitates monitoring for suspicious transactions and attempted \r\n transactions.

\r\n

A process should be implemented for dealing with incomplete documentation \r\n with a view to making it complete and current before doing more \r\n transactions or unrestricted transactions.

\r\n

TRANSACTION REPORTING

\r\n

General

\r\n

The PCMLTFA and PCMLTFR prescribe reporting to FINTRAC on LCTRs, \r\n EFTs, STRs and TPRs.

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that internal reporting processes are designed \r\n to ensure compliance with regulatory reporting requirements as they \r\n relate to transaction reporting systems. Systemic compliance issues \r\n should be documented, escalated to the CAMLO and brought to the \r\n attention of FINTRAC. Control measures should include the identification \r\n of remedial action designed to eliminate compliance issues. For \r\n example, FRFIs should notify FINTRAC promptly of any internally \r\n identified LCTR, EFT or STR reporting errors or omissions. FRFIs \r\n should pay special attention to FINTRAC\xe2\x80\x99s error codes when filing \r\n reports, and take remedial action on a timely basis when FINTRAC \r\n indicates filing errors or other compliance issues. FRFIs should \r\n confirm to FINTRAC when remedial action is complete.

\r\n

Suspicious Transactions and Suspicious Attempted \r\n Transactions, and Reports on Terrorist Property

\r\n

Suspicious transactions and attempted transactions are defined \r\n in the PCMLTFA as those in respect of which there are reasonable \r\n grounds to suspect that the transaction or attempted transaction \r\n is related to the commission or attempted commission of a ML offence \r\n or a TF offence.Footnote 73 \r\n There is no monetary threshold applicable to suspicious transactions \r\n or suspicious attempted transactions.

\r\n

A transaction or attempted transaction that the FRFI reasonably \r\n suspects is related to a money laundering offence must be reported \r\n to FINTRAC. Property in the possession or control of a FRFI that \r\n the FRFIs knows, or has reasonable grounds to believe, is owned \r\n or controlled by or on behalf of a terrorist or a terrorist group, \r\n must also be reported to FINTRAC. This includes information about \r\n any transaction or proposed transaction relating to that property. \r\n

\r\n

The obligation to report suspicious transactions, suspicious attempted \r\n transactions and terrorist propertyFootnote 74 \r\n is designed to assist Canadian law enforcement authorities in their \r\n investigation and prosecution of ML and TF offences and predicate \r\n offences. Robust ongoing monitoring, examination and reporting processes \r\n in FRFIs are crucial in assisting these law enforcement efforts. \r\n

\r\n

Suspicious transactions and suspicious attempted transactions \r\n should be identified by FRFIs from unusual activity or transactions. \r\n Procedures to identify unusual activities should capture the background \r\n and purpose of the transaction(s), who was involved, when and where \r\n it occurred, what products or services were involved and how the \r\n transaction was structured, and should be recorded.

\r\n

STRs must be filed promptly in accordance with the regulatory \r\n requirements. Supporting documentationFootnote 75 \r\n must be retained as prescribed and made available to assist law \r\n enforcement authorities within prescribed deadlines.

\r\n

FRFIs must ensure that information concerning STRs, including \r\n the fact that there is a suspicion and/or an STR, is kept strictly \r\n confidential. The client(s) involved must not be tipped off to a \r\n disclosure, and information within the FRFI must be strictly limited \r\n to the CAMLO and others on a \xe2\x80\x9cneed to know\xe2\x80\x9d basis.

\r\n

The PCMLTFRFootnote 76 \r\n require that, except where identity has been previously ascertained \r\n in accordance with the Regulations, FRFIs must take reasonable measures \r\n to ascertain the identity of every person with whom a suspicious \r\n transaction or suspicious attempted transaction is conducted. While \r\n reasonable measures may include normal client identification practices, \r\n care must be taken to ensure that such practices, if used, do not \r\n have the effect of tipping off the client.

\r\n

Aggregation of Cash Transactions

\r\n

The PCMLTFR providesFootnote 77 \r\n that where two or more cash transactions of less than $10,000 each \r\n are made within 24 consecutive hours that in the aggregate amount \r\n to $10,000 or more, the aggregated transactions are considered to \r\n be a single transaction of $10,000 or more for reporting purposes \r\n if a FRFI knows that the transactions are conducted by, or on behalf \r\n of, the same person or entity, or an employee or a senior officer \r\n of the FRFI knows that the transactions are conducted by, or on \r\n behalf of, the same person or entity.

\r\n

For the purposes of interpreting this requirement, FRFIs that \r\n have systems in place that permit the FRFI to know, by making a \r\n record of multiple cash transactions referred to in this requirement, \r\n that the transactions are conducted by or on behalf of the same \r\n client should ensure that such transactions are aggregated and reported \r\n to FINTRAC as Large Cash Transactions.

\r\n

TRAINING

\r\n

Effective training programs for staff and others (as required) \r\n is an important and statutorily required element of FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 AML/ATF \r\n programs.

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that written AML/ATF training programs are \r\n developed and maintained. Appropriate training should be considered \r\n for the Board, Senior Management, employees, agents and any other \r\n persons who may be responsible for control activity, outcomes or \r\n oversight, or who are authorized to act on the Company\xe2\x80\x99s behalf \r\n pursuant to the PCMLTFRFootnote 78. The nature and content should be appropriate to the \r\n AML/ATF responsibilities of and the FRFI\'s relationship with, each \r\n intended recipient group. In particular, training should be tailored \r\n to provide the types and granularity of information and skills that \r\n are necessary for effective performance of the AML/ATF function \r\n in each case.

\r\n

Training programs for the Board and Senior Management should \r\n provide sufficient briefing with respect to inherent risks and controls \r\n to enable them to assess information reported by the CAMLO and Auditor, \r\n and exercise effective oversight over the AML/ATF program.

\r\n

An effective way of providing training for new members of the \r\n Board is to include an AML/ATF program overview information session \r\n in new director orientation.

\r\n

SELF ASSESSMENT of CONTROLS

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that a self assessment of control measures \r\n is conducted, preferably on an ongoing basis, but at least annually. \r\n The assessment of AML/ATF controls is an important component of \r\n AML/ATF program because of its quality assurance outcome.

\r\n

While the assessments in business areas can and should be conducted \r\n by individuals in those business areas, FRFIs should ensure that \r\n the assessment process is designed to enable results in each area \r\n to be consolidated for analysis and other purposes.

\r\n

The self assessment in each relevant area of the FRFI should cover, \r\n at a minimum, the adequacy of the inherent risk assessment, AML/ATF \r\n policies and procedures, training and other controls implemented \r\n to mitigate ML and TF risks.

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that the self assessment is neither too narrow \r\n nor too broad. For example, a narrow legal/regulatory-based assessment \r\n could fail to cover broader ML and TF controls. Similarly an operational-based \r\n assessment might fail to cover prescribed controls.

\r\n

All significant information used in the self assessment process \r\n should be verified or readily verifiable. Methods used to ensure \r\n that information is verified or verifiable will depend on the size, \r\n complexity and governance structure of the FRFI. Reasonably effective \r\n measures observed by OSFI tend to fall into one or more of the following \r\n categories:

\r\n
    \r\n
  1. Business areas are required by the CAMLO to provide information \r\n on the methodology they used to assess or re-assess ML and TF \r\n controls in their areas, in support of assessment results;
    2. \r\n
  2. Business areas are required by the CAMLO to provide evidence \r\n of having documented support for the results of their assessments; \r\n
    3. \r\n
  3. The CAMLO reviews and confirms the assessment results; or
    4. \r\n
  4. A combination of the above.
    5. \r\n
\r\n

The self assessment of controls should provide FRFIs with:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Insight into the efficacy of controls in the AML/ATF program, \r\n and the overall extent to which the program adequately mitigates \r\n the identified inherent risks of ML and TF; and
    • \r\n
  • Information to aid in prioritizing remediation efforts if controls \r\n are under-performing and opportunities to capture economies of \r\n scale to better allocate resources to areas of higher risk.
    • \r\n
\r\n

EFFECTIVENESS TESTING

\r\n

Like the assessments of inherent risk and risk management controls, \r\n effectiveness testing of the AML/ATF program is an important component \r\n of AML/ATF program quality assurance and is a statutorily required \r\n part of the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s AML/ATF program.

\r\n

The PCMLTFRFootnote 79 \r\n require that the following AML/ATF program components be reviewed \r\n for the purpose of testing their effectiveness every two years: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Policies and Procedures;
    • \r\n
  • Risk assessments;
    • \r\n
  • Training programs.
    • \r\n
\r\n

The PCMLTFRFootnote 80 \r\n also prescribe minimum content and timing for reports to a senior \r\n officer on effectiveness testing.

\r\n

In addition, it would be prudent for FRFIs to ensure that all \r\n other elements of the AML/ATF program be tested for effectiveness \r\n on a similar time scale.

\r\n

FRFIs have access to internal or external auditors (or both) and \r\n therefore FRFIs should ensure that the Auditor is responsible for \r\n effectiveness testing. However, this does not preclude the Auditor \r\n from outsourcing all or part of the effectiveness testing to qualified \r\n third parties, although remaining responsible for the effectiveness \r\n testing program. FRFIs should ensure that effectiveness testing \r\n of the AML/ATF program is included in the Auditor\xe2\x80\x99s mandate and \r\n audit program.

\r\n

Effectiveness testing may be carried out on a stand-alone basis, \r\n or embedded in broader audits with other audit work. Whichever approach \r\n is taken, testing must cover all key AML/ATF program components, \r\n including policies and procedures, risk assessments and training \r\n programs at least every two years.

\r\n

FRFIs should ensure that effectiveness testing is:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • in addition to (not a substitute for) assessments of inherent \r\n risks and risk management controls;
    • \r\n
  • appropriately risk based, with testing more frequently and/or \r\n thoroughly in higher risk categories throughout the FRFI as identified \r\n in the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s Inherent Risk Assessment;
    • \r\n
  • planned and performed by an auditor or auditors who have had \r\n appropriate AML/ATF training and experience in respect of ML and \r\n TF risk and an appropriate level of knowledge of the regulatory \r\n requirements and guidelines; and
    • \r\n
  • reported on to appropriate Senior Management, and the Board, \r\n including information on testing scope, findings and recommendations \r\n or requirements for remedial action, and management\xe2\x80\x99s responses \r\n thereto.
    • \r\n
\r\n
