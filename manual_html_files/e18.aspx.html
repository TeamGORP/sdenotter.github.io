Stress testing is an important tool for senior management to use \r\n in making business strategy, risk management and capital management \r\n decisions. This guideline sets out OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s expectations with respect \r\n to stress testing and applies to banks and bank holding companies, \r\n and to all federally regulated trust and loan companies, cooperative \r\n credit associations, life insurance companies and fraternal benefit \r\n societies, property and casualty insurance companies and insurance \r\n holding companies (collectively referred to as \xe2\x80\x9cinstitutions\xe2\x80\x9d). \r\n

A. Stress Testing Defined

\r\n

Stress testing is a risk management technique used to evaluate \r\n the potential effects on an institution\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition, of \r\n a set of specified changes in risk factors, corresponding to exceptional \r\n but plausible eventsFootnote 1. Stress testing \r\n includes scenario testing and sensitivity testing (refer to Glossary). \r\n

\r\n

Stress testing is especially important after long periods of benign \r\n economic and financial conditions, when fading memory of negative \r\n conditions can lead to complacency and the underpricing of risk. \r\n It is also a key risk management tool during periods of expansion, \r\n when innovation leads to new products that grow rapidly and for \r\n which limited or no historical experience is available.

\r\n

Stress testing attempts to determine the impact of situations \r\n where the assumptions underlying established models used in managing \r\n a business break down. This applies equally to valuation models, \r\n models of individual risks and models that aggregate individual \r\n risks.

\r\n

B. Purposes of Stress Testing

\r\n

Stress testing should be embedded in enterprise wide risk management. \r\n A stress testing program as a whole should be actionable, playing \r\n an important role in facilitating the development of risk mitigation \r\n or contingency plans across a range of stressed conditions. It should \r\n feed into the institution\xe2\x80\x99s decision making process, including setting \r\n the institution\xe2\x80\x99s risk appetite, setting exposure limits, and evaluating \r\n strategic choices in longer term business planning.

\r\n

An institution\xe2\x80\x99s stress testing program should serve the following \r\n purposes:

\r\n
    \r\n
  1. Risk identification and control \xe2\x80\x93 Stress testing \r\n should be included in an institution\xe2\x80\x99s risk management activities \r\n at various levels, for example, ranging from risk mitigation policies \r\n at a detailed or portfolio level to adjusting the institution\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n business strategy. In particular, it should be used to address \r\n institution-wide risks, and consider the concentrations and interactions \r\n between risks in stress environments that might otherwise be overlooked. \r\n
    2. \r\n
  2. Providing a complementary risk perspective to other \r\n risk management tools \xe2\x80\x93 Stress tests should complement \r\n risk quantification methodologies that are based on complex, quantitative \r\n models using backward looking data and estimated statistical relationships. \r\n In particular, stress testing outcomes for a particular portfolio \r\n can provide insights about the validity of statistical models \r\n at high confidence intervals, for example those used to determine \r\n VaR. \r\n

    As stress testing allows for the simulation of shocks which \r\n have not previously occurred, it should be used to assess the \r\n robustness of models to possible changes in the economic and \r\n financial environment. Stress tests should help to detect vulnerabilities \r\n such as unidentified risk concentrations or potential interactions \r\n between types of risk that could threaten the viability of the \r\n institution, but may be concealed when relying purely on statistical \r\n risk management tools based on historical data.

    \r\n

    Stress testing can also be used to assess the impacts of customer \r\n behaviour arising from options embedded in certain products \r\n \xe2\x80\x93 particularly where the impact is not easily modelled under \r\n extreme events.

    \r\n
    3. \r\n
  3. Supporting capital management \xe2\x80\x93 Stress testing \r\n should form an integral part of institutions\xe2\x80\x99 internal capital \r\n management where rigorous, forward-looking stress testing can \r\n identify severe events, including a series of compounding events, \r\n or changes in market conditions that could adversely impact the \r\n institution.
    4. \r\n
  4. Improving liquidity management \xe2\x80\x93 Stress testing \r\n should be a central tool in identifying, measuring and controlling \r\n funding liquidity risks, in particular for assessing the institution\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n liquidity profile and the adequacy of liquidity buffers in case \r\n of both institution-specific and market-wide stress events.
    5. \r\n
\r\n

C. Role of the BoardFootnote 2 and Senior \r\n Management

\r\n

Board and senior management involvement in the stress testing \r\n program is essential for its effective operation. The board has \r\n ultimate responsibility for the overall stress testing program and \r\n should be aware of the key findings from stress tests. Senior management \r\n is accountable for the program\xe2\x80\x99s implementation, management and \r\n oversight and for ensuring that the institution has adequate plans \r\n to deal with remote but plausible stress scenarios.

\r\n

The board must ensure that its senior management has in place \r\n a \xe2\x80\x9cfit for purpose\xe2\x80\x9d program that is enterprise wide and that management \r\n has adopted policies requiring appropriate use of stress testing \r\n as a management tool.

\r\n

Senior management should be able to identify and clearly articulate \r\n the institution\xe2\x80\x99s risk appetite and understand the impact of stress \r\n events on the risk profile of the institution. Senior management \r\n must participate in the review and identification of potential stress \r\n scenarios, as well as contribute to the development and implementation \r\n of risk mitigation strategies. In addition, senior management should \r\n consider an appropriate number of well-understood, documented, utilised \r\n and sufficiently severe scenarios that are relevant to their institution. \r\n

\r\n

Senior management\xe2\x80\x99s endorsement of stress testing as a guide in \r\n decision-making is particularly valuable when the tests reveal vulnerabilities \r\n that the institution finds costly to address or difficult to resolve \r\n in a timely, appropriate and realistic manner.

\r\n

D. General Considerations for Stress Testing \r\n Programs

\r\n

Stress testing programs should take account of views from \r\n across the organisation and should cover a range of perspectives \r\n and techniques.

\r\n

The identification of relevant stress events, the application \r\n of sound modelling approaches and the appropriate use of stress \r\n testing results each require the collaboration of different senior \r\n experts such as risk controllers, economists, business managers, \r\n traders and actuaries. Institutions should also use a range of techniques \r\n in order to achieve comprehensive coverage in their stress testing \r\n program, including quantitative and qualitative techniques to support \r\n and complement models and to extend stress testing to areas where \r\n effective risk management requires greater use of judgement.

\r\n

Institutions should have written policies and procedures governing \r\n the stress testing program. The operation of the program should \r\n be appropriately documented.

\r\n

The assumptions and fundamental elements for each stress testing \r\n exercise should be appropriately documented, including the reasoning \r\n and judgements underlying the scenarios chosen and the sensitivity \r\n of stress testing results to the range and severity of the scenarios. \r\n The level of documentation should be based on the nature and purposes \r\n of the stress testing. For example, documentation of ad hoc sensitivity \r\n tests for tactical decisions may be less elaborate than the documentation \r\n of enterprise-wide stress tests used for strategic decision making. \r\n An evaluation of fundamental assumptions should be performed regularly \r\n or in light of changing external conditions. The results of the \r\n assessments should also be documented.

\r\n

An institution should have a suitably robust infrastructure \r\n in place, which is sufficiently flexible to accommodate different \r\n and possibly changing stress tests at an appropriate level of granularity. \r\n

\r\n

The infrastructure should be able to aggregate comparable risks \r\n and exposures across the institution. It should allow for reporting \r\n to senior management and the board in a timely manner throughout \r\n the fiscal year. The infrastructure and information systems should \r\n be sufficiently flexible to accommodate a timely increase in the \r\n frequency of ad hoc sensitivity testing to support senior management\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n response to rapid changes in the operating environment and also \r\n for purposes of responding to the concerns of external stakeholders \r\n and regulators.

\r\n

An institution\xe2\x80\x99s stress testing infrastructure and information \r\n systems should be commensurate with the nature and complexity of \r\n the institution and its risk profile. For example, greater risk \r\n factor volatility and shorter time horizons for management actions \r\n require infrastructure and information systems that accommodate \r\n more frequent stress testing in those areas.

\r\n

An institution should regularly maintain and update its stress \r\n testing framework. The effectiveness of the stress testing program, \r\n as well as the robustness of individual components, should be assessed \r\n regularly and independently.

\r\n

Assessments of effectiveness should be qualitative as well as \r\n quantitative, given the importance of judgments and the severity \r\n of shocks considered. Areas for assessment should include effectiveness \r\n of the program in meeting its intended purposes, documentation, \r\n development work, system implementation, management oversight, data \r\n quality and hypotheses and assumptions used.

\r\n

Since the stress test development and maintenance processes often \r\n imply judgmental and expert decisions (e.g. assumptions to be tested, \r\n calibration of the stress, etc.), the independent control functions \r\n such as risk management and internal audit should also play a key \r\n role in the process. In particular there should be an independent \r\n review (e.g., by internal audit) of the adequacy of the design and \r\n effectiveness of the operations of an institution\xe2\x80\x99s stress testing \r\n programs.

\r\n

E. Methodology and Scenario Selection

\r\n

Stress tests should cover a range of risks and business areas, \r\n as well as at the institution-wide level. An institution should \r\n be able to integrate effectively, in a meaningful fashion, across \r\n the range of its stress testing activities to deliver a complete \r\n picture of institution-wide risk.

\r\n

A stress testing program should consistently and comprehensively \r\n cover product-, business- and entity-specific views. Using a level \r\n of granularity appropriate to the purpose of the stress test, stress \r\n testing programs should examine the effect of shocks across all \r\n relevant risk factors, taking into account interrelations among \r\n them.

\r\n

Comprehensive stress testing programs should consider the institution\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n most material and significant risks. Where relevant and material, \r\n such risks may include:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • credit risk, including counterparty and reinsurance risk \r\n
    • \r\n
  • market risk, e.g., \r\n
      \r\n
    • general market
      • \r\n
    • specific
      • \r\n
    • cash flow mismatch
      • \r\n
    • interest rate
      • \r\n
    • foreign exchange
      • \r\n
    • commodity
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • insurance risk, e.g., \r\n
      \r\n
    • mortality
      • \r\n
    • morbidity
      • \r\n
    • claim frequency and severity
      • \r\n
    • persistency and lapse risk
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    • \r\n
  • liquidity risk
    • \r\n
  • operational and legal risk
    • \r\n
  • concentration risk
    • \r\n
  • contagion risk
    • \r\n
  • risk to reputation
    • \r\n
  • securitization risk
    • \r\n
  • new business risk
    • \r\n
  • regulatory risk
    • \r\n
  • inflation risk
    • \r\n
\r\n

The impact of stress tests is usually evaluated using one or more \r\n measures. The particular measures used will depend on the specific \r\n purpose of the stress test, the risks and portfolios being analysed \r\n and the particular issue under examination. A range of measures \r\n may need to be considered to convey an adequate impression of the \r\n impact. Typical measures used are:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • asset and liability values
    • \r\n
  • level of impaired assets and write-offs
    • \r\n
  • accounting profit and loss
    • \r\n
  • economic profit and loss
    • \r\n
  • required and available regulatory capital
    • \r\n
  • economic capital
    • \r\n
  • liquidity and funding gaps
    • \r\n
\r\n

Stress testing programs should apply across business and product \r\n lines and cover a range of scenarios, including non-historical scenarios, \r\n and aim to take into account system-wide interactions and feedback \r\n effects (e.g., second order and macroeconomic effects).

\r\n

Stress tests should be conducted flexibly and imaginatively, in \r\n order to improve the likelihood of identifying hidden vulnerabilities. \r\n A \xe2\x80\x9cfailure of imagination\xe2\x80\x9d could lead to an underestimation of the \r\n likelihood and severity of extreme events and to a false sense of \r\n security about an institution\xe2\x80\x99s resilience.

\r\n

The institution should assess the impact of severe shocks and \r\n periods of severe and sustained downturns, including its ability \r\n to react over the time horizon appropriate for the business and \r\n risks being tested.

\r\n

Institutions should use stress tests to identify, monitor and \r\n control risk concentrations. To adequately address risk concentrations, \r\n the scenario should to be firm-wide and comprehensive, covering \r\n balance sheet and off-balance sheet assets, contingent and non- \r\n contingent risks, and should give due consideration to actions beyond \r\n contractual obligations that might be undertaken to preserve reputation. \r\n Further, stress tests should identify and respond to potential changes \r\n in market conditions that could adversely impact an institution\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n exposure to risk concentrations.

\r\n

Stress tests should feature a range of severities, including \r\n events capable of generating the most damage, whether through size \r\n of loss or through loss of reputation. A stress testing program \r\n should also determine what scenarios could challenge the viability \r\n of the institution (reverse stress tests). Such tests may be useful \r\n in uncovering hidden risks and interactions among risks.

\r\n

Stress tests should be geared towards events and business areas \r\n that might be particularly damaging for the institution. Areas which \r\n benefit in particular from the use of stress testing are business \r\n lines where traditional risk management models indicate an exceptionally \r\n good risk/return trade-off; new products and new markets which have \r\n not experienced severe strains; and exposures where there are no \r\n liquid two way markets.

\r\n

Institutions should conduct reverse stress tests. A reverse stress \r\n test starts with a specified outcome that challenges the viability \r\n of the firm. One example of such an outcome would be that over a \r\n short time period, the firm incurs a very large loss that challenges \r\n its viability. The analysis would then work backward (reverse engineered) \r\n to identify a scenario or combination of scenarios that could bring \r\n about such a specified outcome. The reverse stress test induces \r\n institutions to consider scenarios beyond normal business settings \r\n that would include events with contagion and systemic implications. \r\n

\r\n

As part of an overall stress testing program, a deposit-taking \r\n institution should aim to take account of simultaneous pressures \r\n in funding and asset markets, and the impact of a reduction in market \r\n liquidity on asset valuation. Funding and asset markets may be strongly \r\n interrelated, particularly during periods of stress. An institution \r\n should enhance its stress testing practices by considering important \r\n interrelations between various factors, including price shocks for \r\n specific asset categories; the drying-up of corresponding asset \r\n liquidity; the possibility of significant losses damaging the institution\xe2\x80\x99s \r\n financial strength; growth of liquidity needs as a consequence of \r\n liquidity commitments; taking on board affected assets; and diminished \r\n access to secured or unsecured funding markets.3

\r\n

As part of an overall stress testing program at an insurance company, \r\n specific consideration should be given to important interrelations \r\n between various risk factors. For a life insurer, changes in economic \r\n conditions can significantly affect policyholder behaviour such \r\n as lapse rates, utilization of options within an insurance contract, \r\n and morbidity and recovery rates. For a property and casualty insurer, \r\n changing economic conditions will not only influence investment \r\n income and company expenses, but can also, particularly in times \r\n of inflation, lead to higher claims and loss reserves. The interrelations \r\n of various factors will depend upon the insurer\xe2\x80\x99s products, its \r\n investment policy and its approach to managing its business. A critical \r\n goal for insurers is to identify situations in which the assumed \r\n normal pattern of interrelationships breaks down due to a change \r\n in the business environment.

\r\n

F. Specific Areas of Focus

\r\n

The following risks have proven to require specific attention \r\n in light of experience of financial market turmoil:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • Risk Mitigation
    • \r\n
  • Securitization and Warehousing Risks
    • \r\n
  • Risks to Reputation
    • \r\n
  • Counterparty Credit Risk
    • \r\n
  • Risk Concentrations
    • \r\n
\r\n

As such, stress testing should be prominent among the risk assessment \r\n tools used where these specific risks are material.

\r\n

Risk Mitigation

\r\n

Stress testing should facilitate the development of risk mitigation \r\n or contingency plans across a range of stressed conditions. The \r\n performance of risk mitigating techniques, like reinsurance, hedging, \r\n netting and the use of collateral, should be challenged and assessed \r\n systematically under stressed conditions when markets may not be \r\n fully functioning and multiple institutions simultaneously could \r\n be pursuing similar risk mitigating strategies.

\r\n

Stress testing should also reflect constraints on management action \r\n and should not place undue reliance on the timeliness of mitigating \r\n actions.

\r\n

Securitization and Warehousing Risks

\r\n

The stress testing program should explicitly cover complex and \r\n customized products such as securitized exposures. Stress tests \r\n for securitized assets should consider the underlying assets, their \r\n exposure to systemic market factors, relevant contractual arrangements \r\n and embedded triggers, and the impact of leverage, particularly \r\n as it relates to the subordination level in the issue structure. \r\n

\r\n

The stress testing program should cover pipeline and warehousing \r\n risks. These are market, credit and funding risks arising in the \r\n period prior to securitization or sale and which may arise from \r\n the need to hold assets for longer periods than originally planned \r\n when markets are disrupted. An institution should include such exposures \r\n in its stress tests regardless of their probability of being securitized. \r\n Many of the risks associated with pipeline and warehoused exposures \r\n emerge when an institution is unable to access the securitization \r\n or other markets due to either institution specific or market stresses. \r\n

\r\n

Risks to Reputation

\r\n

An institution should enhance its stress testing methodologies \r\n to capture the effect of risks to reputation. To mitigate reputational \r\n spill-over effects and maintain market confidence, an institution \r\n should have an approach to assess the impact of risks to reputation \r\n on other risk types.

\r\n

The institution should integrate risks arising from off-balance \r\n sheet vehicles and other related entities in its stress testing \r\n program. An institution should carefully assess the risks associated \r\n with commitments to off-balance sheet vehicles related to structured \r\n credit securities and the possibility that assets will need to be \r\n taken on balance sheet for reputational reasons. Therefore, in its \r\n stress testing program, an institution should include scenarios \r\n assessing the size and soundness of such vehicles relative to its \r\n own financial, liquidity and regulatory capital positions. This \r\n analysis should include structural, solvency, liquidity and other \r\n risk issues, including the effects of covenants and triggers.

\r\n

Counterparty Credit risk

\r\n

An institution may have large gross exposures to leveraged counterparties, \r\n including hedge funds, financial guarantors, investment banks and \r\n derivatives counterparties that may be particularly exposed to specific \r\n asset types and market movements. Under normal conditions, these \r\n exposures are typically completely secured by posted collateral \r\n and continuous re-margining agreements yielding zero or very small \r\n net exposures. In the case of severe market shocks, however, these \r\n exposures may increase abruptly. The potential cross- correlation \r\n of the creditworthiness of derivative counterparties with the risks \r\n of the reference assets may emerge (i.e., wrong-way risk). An institution \r\n should ensure that its stress testing approaches related to derivative \r\n counterparties are robust in their capture of such correlated tail \r\n risks.

\r\n

Risk Concentrations

\r\n

Stress testing should consider risk concentrations resulting directly \r\n from risk taking activities as well as those resulting indirectly \r\n from actions to mitigate risks, e.g., concentrations of credit counterparty \r\n risk arising from hedges of market and insurance risk.

\r\n

Risk concentrations may arise along different dimensions:

\r\n
    \r\n
  • single name concentrations
    • \r\n
  • concentrations in regions or industries
    • \r\n
  • concentrations in single risk factors
    • \r\n
  • concentrations in indirect exposures via posted collateral or \r\n hedge positions
    • \r\n
  • concentrations in off-balance sheet exposure, contingent exposure \r\n or non-contractual obligations by reputational reasons
    • \r\n
\r\n

In addition, concentrations may arise based on correlated risk \r\n factors that reflect subtler or more situation-specific factors, \r\n such as previously undetected correlations between market and credit \r\n risks, as well as between those risks and liquidity risk.

\r\n

G. Supervisory Considerations

\r\n

OSFI reviews institutions\xe2\x80\x99 stress testing programs as part of \r\n the supervisory review process as described in the Supervisory Framework, \r\n and as part of its review of a deposit-taking

\r\n

institution\xe2\x80\x99s Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP). \r\n For insurers, one example of stress testing is Dynamic Capital Adequacy \r\n Testing (DCAT). OSFI expects to see evidence that stress testing \r\n is integrated into institutions\xe2\x80\x99 internal risk management processes. \r\n

\r\n

OSFI uses the results of institutions\xe2\x80\x99 stress testing programs \r\n as important information and integrates the results into its assessment \r\n of the inherent risks and risk controls and oversight of institutions\xe2\x80\x99 \r\n business activities

\r\n

In assessing institutions\xe2\x80\x99 stress testing programs, OSFI may: \r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  1. Evaluate whether scenarios chosen are consistent with the risk \r\n appetite the institution has set for itself.
    2. \r\n
  2. Assess whether scenarios are appropriate to the portfolio of \r\n the institution and that they include severe shocks and periods \r\n of severe and sustained downturn. The scenarios chosen should \r\n also include, where relevant, an episode of market turbulence \r\n or a shock to market liquidity.
    3. \r\n
  3. Assess whether the frequency and timing of stress testing is \r\n sufficient to support timely management action. For example, stress \r\n testing and DCAT are complementary initiatives. More frequent \r\n stress testing at the business unit level facilitates timely reaction \r\n to sudden market developments. It also supports the integration \r\n of the DCAT process with the finalization of an annual business \r\n plan by providing timely inputs based on current information. \r\n While it is up to each institution to determine how to best integrate \r\n DCAT and other stress testing into its business planning process \r\n to achieve the maximum benefits, ideally the annual DCAT of an \r\n insurance company would be available to the board as soon as is \r\n reasonably possible; in all cases the annual DCAT should be submitted \r\n to OSFI within 30 days of its presentation to the board.
    4. \r\n
  4. Ask institutions to evaluate scenarios under which viability \r\n is compromised and may ask institutions to test scenarios specific \r\n to different lines of business, to assess the plausibility of \r\n events that could materialize in significant strategic or reputational \r\n risk, in particular for business lines with significant balance \r\n sheet exposures.
    5. \r\n
  5. Ask institutions, from time to time, to carry out standardized: \r\n
      \r\n
    • sensitivity tests for individual businesses/products given \r\n evolving market conditions or
      • \r\n
    • scenario tests for use by OSFI to assess system wide vulnerabilities. \r\n
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    6. \r\n
  6. Examine the future capital resources and capital requirements \r\n under adverse scenarios. In particular, OSFI would consider the \r\n results of forward-looking stress testing for assessing the adequacy \r\n of capital buffers.
    7. \r\n
  7. Take account of the extent to which capital might not be freely \r\n transferable within groups under adverse scenarios. OSFI would \r\n also consider the possibility a crisis impairs the ability of \r\n even very healthy institutions to raise funds at reasonable cost. \r\n
    8. \r\n
  8. Review the range of management actions envisaged by institutions \r\n in response to the results of the stress testing exercise and \r\n be able to understand the rationale for the management body decision \r\n to take or not to take remedial actions. Supervisors may challenge \r\n whether such actions will be available in a period of stress and \r\n whether the institution will realistically be able and willing \r\n to take such actions.
    9. \r\n
  9. Make recommendations to an institution to take appropriate \r\n remedial action to address weaknesses in its stress testing program. \r\n
    10. \r\n
\r\n

From time to time, OSFI may conduct an analysis of the impact \r\n of system-wide stress scenarios. OSFI intends as much as possible \r\n to test the impact of these system-wide scenarios using information \r\n that is reported in regulatory returns or regularly collected as \r\n part of the supervisory review process in order to minimize data \r\n calls on institutions.

\r\n

Glossary

\r\n

Scenario testing:

\r\n

Scenario testing uses a hypothetical future state of the world \r\n to define changes in risk factors affecting an institution\xe2\x80\x99s operations. \r\n This will normally involve changes in a number of risk factors, \r\n as well as ripple effects that are other impacts that follow logically \r\n from these changes and related management and regulatory actions. \r\n Scenario testing is typically conducted over the time horizon appropriate \r\n for the business and risks being tested.

\r\n

Sensitivity testing:

\r\n

Sensitivity testing typically involves an incremental change in \r\n a risk factor (or a limited number of risk factors). It is typically \r\n conducted over a shorter time horizon, for example an instantaneous \r\n shock. Sensitivity testing requires fewer resources than scenario \r\n testing and can be used as a simpler technique for assessing the \r\n impact of a change in risks when a quick response or when more frequent \r\n results are needed.

\r\n
\r\n \r\n \xc2\xa0
\r\n

Board:

\r\n

Board refers to the institution\xe2\x80\x99s board of directors. Where foreign \r\n insurance companies or foreign banks operate in Canada on a branch \r\n basis, OSFI looks to the Chief Agent or Principal Officer of a branch \r\n to oversee the management of the branch, including matters of corporate \r\n governance.
