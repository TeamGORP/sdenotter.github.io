\r\n I. Introduction\r\n

\r\n

\r\n This Guideline is intended to assist federally regulated financial\r\n institutionsFootnote 1 (FRFIs) in establishing,\r\n as required by the relevant provisions of FRFI legislation,Footnote 2 policies regarding the creation of\r\n security interests in their propertyFootnote 3 and the acquisition of beneficial interests in property that is\r\n subject to security interests, hereafter referred to as \xe2\x80\x9cpledging.\xe2\x80\x9d This\r\n is not a Guideline pursuant to statutory authority found in FRFI\r\n legislation; therefore, it does not have force of law.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n The Guideline outlines factors that OSFI expects the FRFI\'s board of\r\n directors or committee of the board to consider when establishing pledging\r\n policies. These factors are aimed at ensuring that a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s policies\r\n reflect its business operations and associated risks and that the policies\r\n are implemented effectively.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n OSFI expects that FRFIs will also develop pledging policies on an\r\n enterprise-wide basis, in a manner consistent with their overall liquidity\r\n and other risk management practices.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n The Guideline will be applied when the new legislative provisions on\r\n pledging come into force. These provisions will require that the directors\r\n of a FRFI establish pledging policies, and that the FRFI adheres to those\r\n policies. However, the requirements will not apply in respect of pledging\r\n by an insurance company or deposit-taking FRFI to secure an obligation to\r\n the Bank of Canada or, in the case of a deposit-taking FRFI, to the Canada\r\n Deposit Insurance Corporation.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n II. Definition\r\n

\r\n

\r\n FRFI legislation defines \xe2\x80\x9csecurity interest\xe2\x80\x9d as an interest in or charge\r\n on property by way of mortgage, lien, pledge or otherwise taken by a\r\n creditor or guarantor to secure the payment or performance of an\r\n obligation. Although certain activities, such as securities lending, may\r\n result in the creation of security interests, actually determining whether\r\n a specific activity or transaction results in pledging rests on the\r\n particulars of the transaction.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n III. Background\r\n

\r\n

\r\n OSFI recognizes that pledging is an integral part of a number of\r\n activities or transactions that are important to FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 operations. Some\r\n of those activities or transactions, such as liquidity management, use of\r\n derivatives and securities lending, may themselves be subject to other\r\n OSFI guidelines. The objective of pledging by a FRFI is generally to\r\n secure its own financial obligations. However, pledging is also part of a\r\n number of activities that reduce risk to the FRFI and to the financial\r\n system. For example, pledging may be used in support of a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s\r\n derivative contracts to hedge interest, currency or other market risks. As\r\n well, pledging requirements associated with participation in certain\r\n systems, such as clearing houses and payment systems, can help contribute\r\n to reducing systemic risk.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n Pledging can affect a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s ability to meet its liquidity requirements.\r\n For example, in the event of a liquidity crisis, a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s ability to\r\n borrow money may hinge upon the availability of unencumbered assetsFootnote 4. Therefore, OFSI expects FRFIs to\r\n consider liquidity risk in establishing their pledging policies. Pledging\r\n policies can also affect unsecured creditors, because the higher the\r\n proportion of pledged property, the fewer the assets available for\r\n unsecured creditors (i.e., depositors, policyholders and other creditors)\r\n in the event of a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s insolvency.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n OSFI expects FRFIs\xe2\x80\x99 pledging policies to reflect an appropriate balance\r\n between the need to conduct their business and compete, the responsibility\r\n to effectively manage the risks related to their operations, and the\r\n interests of their unsecured creditors. In this context, information\r\n relating to pledged assets is important from a liquidity management\r\n standpoint and, in the event of insolvency, becomes critical for\r\n depositors, policyholders and other unsecured creditors. Identifying\r\n business activity categories that require pledging and reporting pledged\r\n assets are key elements of a FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s pledging policies.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n IV. Pledging Policies\r\n

\r\n

\r\n OSFI expects FRFIs to establish and implement pledging policies that are\r\n approved by the board of directors or a committee of the board. These\r\n policies establish the framework within which pledging can occur in\r\n relation to various business activities. Further, OSFI expects that the\r\n board or board committee will review the policies at least annually and\r\n approve significant changes to them. When approving such policies, OSFI\r\n expects that the board or board committee will take into account factors\r\n such as the institution\xe2\x80\x99s size, the nature and complexity of its\r\n operations, its overall risk philosophy, and its ability to monitor and\r\n control transactions that require pledging. Where it is appropriate to do\r\n so, the policies may specify the parameters for delegation of certain\r\n responsibilities and functions necessary to the implementation of some of\r\n the elements set out below.\r\n

\r\n

\r\n A FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s pledging policies should encompass the following elements:\r\n

\r\n
    \r\n
  1. \r\n

    \r\n An outline of the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s objectives when engaging in activities that\r\n require pledging, having regard to the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s policy towards risk and\r\n risk management, including its tolerance of risk.\r\n

    \r\n
    2. \r\n
  2. \r\n

    \r\n A description of the business reasons (e.g., hedging, liquidity\r\n management, access to markets, support for its core operations) for\r\n the activities or transactions that require pledging. Examples of such\r\n activities or transactions include:\r\n

    \r\n
      \r\n
    • \r\n participation in securities depositories and various clearing and\r\n settlement systems (including systems for payments and foreign\r\n exchange);\r\n
      • \r\n
    • \r\n derivatives operations (e.g., pledging to counterparties in respect of\r\n over the counter derivatives operations);\r\n
      • \r\n
    • \r\n borrowing on a secured basis (e.g., mortgaging of property);\r\n
      • \r\n
    • \r\n securities borrowing/lending;\r\n
      • \r\n
    • \r\n reinsurance; and\r\n
      • \r\n
    • \r\n capital equivalency deposits (e.g., to satisfy the requirements of a\r\n foreign government).\r\n
      • \r\n
    \r\n
    3. \r\n
  3. \r\n

    \r\n Appropriate limits on pledging for particular business activities,\r\n having regard to whether the value of the assets pledged is reasonable\r\n in relation to the benefits that the FRFI can expect to derive from\r\n the activity or transaction that requires pledging. Such limits are\r\n particularly important when the activity does not involve risk\r\n mitigation. The level of pledging for particular activities and the\r\n aggregate level of pledging for the institution, and on an\r\n enterprise-wide basis, should be assessed with respect to their\r\n significance to the FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s liquidity requirements and policies, the\r\n strength of its capital and its ability to absorb losses, and to the\r\n potential effect that the pledging may have on unsecured creditors,\r\n including depositors and policyholders.\r\n

    \r\n
    4. \r\n
  4. \r\n

    \r\n Quantitative limits on an individual officer\xe2\x80\x99s ability to execute\r\n agreements or approve transactions that require pledging, taking into\r\n account the standards that the FRFI has in place (see item 3). Each\r\n FRFI should determine the circumstances where individual (per officer)\r\n transaction limits as well as aggregate limits would be appropriate.\r\n

    \r\n
    5. \r\n
  5. \r\n

    \r\n Measures aimed at monitoring the value of assets pledged in various\r\n activities or transactions, as well as the aggregate value of assets\r\n pledged, and the performance of appropriate stress tests to determine\r\n any additional collateral the FRFI may have to pledge under various\r\n scenarios (e.g., market movements or triggers such as rating\r\n downgrades).\r\n

    \r\n
    6. \r\n
  6. \r\n

    \r\n The basis on which reporting and monitoring of pledging should be\r\n performed, having regard to the type and nature of the operation and\r\n including other factors such as whether contracts are subject to\r\n novation or other legally valid forms of netting. Reporting should\r\n include information relating to pledged assets and unencumbered\r\n assets.\r\n

    \r\n
    7. \r\n
  7. \r\n

    \r\n The frequency and format of internal reporting for monitoring\r\n adherence to the policies.\r\n

    \r\n
    8. \r\n
  8. \r\n

    \r\n Appropriate practices and procedures to ensure that the policies are\r\n respected. For instance, although individual departments engaging in\r\n activities or transactions that require pledging are expected to have\r\n proper controls in place for these operations, ultimate responsibility\r\n for the oversight of all such operations is expected to reside in a\r\n single, clearly defined department or individual that is independent\r\n of the departments where the operations originate.\r\n

    \r\n
    9. \r\n
  9. \r\n

    \r\n A requirement that regular (at least annual) board (or board\r\n committee) reviews be carried out of established policies and of the\r\n FRFI\xe2\x80\x99s adherence to the policies.\r\n

    \r\n
    10. \r\n
\r\n

\r\n Where FRFIs have other internal policies in relation to activities or\r\n transactions that require pledging, these policies should be included (or\r\n referred to) in their pledging policies.\r\n
