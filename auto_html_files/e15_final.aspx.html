Introduction

\r\n\r\n

This Guideline describes the role of the Appointed Actuary in federally regulated insurance companiesFootnote 1 and sets out some of OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s expectations with respect to that role. The Guideline is divided into three parts. The first part summarizes the major responsibilities of the actuary as described in the Insurance Companies Act (ICA) and the related Guidelines and Memoranda published by OSFIFootnote 2. The second part deals with the actuary\xe2\x80\x99s qualifications required to carry out the Appointed Actuary\xe2\x80\x99s role, and the third part sets out OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s expectations with respect to peer review of the Appointed Actuary\xe2\x80\x99s work and reports.

\r\n\r\n

Other OSFI Guidelines and MemorandaFootnote 3 contain additional information related to the responsibilities of the Appointed Actuary. Particularly important in this regard is the annual Memorandum to the Appointed Actuary. OSFI issues separate versions of the Memorandum to actuaries of life insurance companies and to actuaries of property and casualty insurance companies.

Section 1: Legal Requirements of the Appointed Actuary

\r\n\r\n

This part summarizes a number of sections in the ICA (the applicable sections of the ICA are\r\nshown in brackets) that relate to the appointment and role of the Appointed Actuary, as well as\r\nkey provisions of related Guidelines and Memoranda published by OSFI. It also sets out the\r\nSuperintendent\xe2\x80\x99s expectations regarding the annual reporting of companies\xe2\x80\x99 expected future\r\nfinancial condition. The ICA refers to the actuary of the company. In this Guideline, in keeping\r\nwith common usage in the insurance industry, this person is referred to as the Appointed Actuary\r\n(AA).

\r\n\r\n

a. Appointment of an Actuary

\r\n\r\n

Each companyFootnote 4 must appoint an actuary of the company (49(1), 165(2)(i), 623(1), 660(1)(a)) and\r\nnotify the Superintendent, in writing, of the appointment (357, 623(2)). The AA must be a\r\nFellow of the Canadian Institute of Actuaries (FCIA) (2(1)).

\r\n\r\n

The chief executive officer or the chief operating officer (or a person performing like functions)\r\nof a Canadian company or society or of a provincial company, or the chief agent of a foreign\r\ncompany, may not be appointed as AA unless authorized in writing by the Superintendent\r\n(359.1(1), 624.1(1)). In the case of a Canadian or provincial company, the chief financial officer\r\nor a person performing like functions may not be appointed as AA unless the audit committee of\r\nthe company has provided a written statement to the Superintendent and the appointment is\r\nauthorized by the Superintendent (359.2).

\r\n\r\n

The directors of a company or, in case of a foreign company, the company itself, may revoke the\r\nappointment of the AA. If this happens, the company must notify the Superintendent, in writing,\r\nof the revocation (360, 625). An AA who resigns or whose appointment is revoked shall submit\r\nto the Superintendent and to the directors of a Canadian or provincial company or to the chief\r\nagent of a foreign company a written statement that includes the circumstances and reasons for\r\nthe resignation or why, in the actuary\'s opinion, the appointment was revoked (363, 627(1)).

\r\n\r\n

Where an AA resigns or their appointment is revoked, no person shall accept an appointment or\r\nconsent to be appointed as AA before requesting and receiving from the previous company\r\nactuary the written statement that was submitted to the directors or the chief agent and to the\r\nSuperintendent (364(1), 627(2)). An appointment may be accepted if no reply is received within\r\nfifteen days after a request was made (364(2), 627(3)).

\r\n\r\n

b. Role and Duties of the Appointed Actuary

\r\n\r\n

The AA is required to value the actuarial and other policy liabilities as at the end of a financial\r\nyear, and any other matters specified by the Superintendent. The AA\xe2\x80\x99s valuation is required to\r\nbe in accordance with accepted actuarial practice, with such changes and any additional\r\ndirections that may be made by the Superintendent (365, 629). The AA\xe2\x80\x99s report in the annual\r\nfinancial statement must opine that the policy liabilities are valued in accordance with accepted\r\nactuarial practice (367). It is expected that the valuation includes the selection of appropriate\r\nassumptions and methods, where each separate assumption is expected to be appropriate. It\r\nshould be noted that the Superintendent may appoint an actuary to value certain liabilities or\r\nother matters if the Superintendent is of the opinion that it is necessary (365.1(1), 629.1(1)).

\r\n\r\n

The liabilities shown in the annual return are required to include as a reserve the value of the\r\nactuarial and other policy liabilities (667(1)). The AA is required to make, and the company to\r\nfile with its annual return, the Appointed Actuary\xe2\x80\x99s Report (AAR) on the policy liabilities and on\r\nany other matters the Superintendent may specify in a form determined by the Superintendent\r\n(667(2)). OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s annual Memorandum to the Appointed Actuary contains up-to-date\r\ninstructions on the form and content of this report. Also, the AA shall, not less than twenty-one\r\ndays before the date of the annual meeting of a Canadian or provincial company, make a report\r\non the valuation of the liabilities to the shareholders and policyholders. The AA shall state\r\nwhether, in the AA\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, the annual statement presents fairly the results of the valuation\r\n(367).

\r\n\r\n

The AA is also required in each financial year to meet with and report to the directors or the\r\nchief agent on the company\xe2\x80\x99s financial position. When directed by the Superintendent, the AA\r\nmust also report on the company\xe2\x80\x99s expected future financial condition (368, 630). A report on\r\nthe company\'s expected future financial condition is normally prepared as described in the\r\nCanadian Institute of Actuaries (CIA) standard of practice on Dynamic Capital Adequacy\r\nTesting (DCAT).

\r\n\r\n

The Superintendent expects that a report on the company\xe2\x80\x99s expected future financial condition\r\nwill be prepared annually in compliance with the CIA standards. The DCAT report should be\r\npresented to the directors of the company, or where the directors so choose, with an appropriate\r\nsubcommittee of the board (e.g. audit committee, risk committee, etc.). A DCAT report shall be\r\nbased on the prior year end financial position or a more recent position. If the DCAT report is\r\npresented to the board of directors in the second half of the financial year, then it shall include\r\nmaterial changes in experience and in financial position up to the period of 90 days before the\r\ndate of presentation. It is expected that the projection period for studies of life companies will be\r\nfor at least five years and for P&C companies will be for at least three years. A copy of the\r\nreport will be filed with OSFI within thirty days of presentation to the company\xe2\x80\x99s directors, but\r\nno later than the end of the calendar year.

\r\n\r\n

In addition to the DCAT, the AA is required to report, in writing, to the chief executive officer\r\nand the chief financial officer or to the chief agent of the company any matters that, in the AA\xe2\x80\x99s\r\nopinion, have material adverse effects on the financial condition of the company and that require rectification. The AA must supply a copy of this report to the board of directors. Where, in the\r\nopinion of the AA, suitable action is not being taken to rectify these matters, the AA shall send a\r\ncopy of the report to the Superintendent and advise the directors or the chief agent of the\r\ncompany that this has been done (369, 631).

\r\n\r\n

When a company maintains a participating account (456), the directors of a company must\r\nestablish a policy for determining the dividends and bonuses to be paid to the participating\r\npolicyholders and a policy respecting the management of each of the participating accounts\r\n(165(2)(e)) and (165(2)(e.1)). The AA is required to report to the directors in writing on the\r\nfairness to participating policyholders of any policy established or amended under 165(2)(e) and\r\n165(2)(e.1) and report at least once a year on its continuing fairness (165(3.1) and 165(3.2)).

\r\n\r\n

The AA is required to report, in writing, to the directors on the fairness to participating\r\npolicyholders of a proposed dividend, bonus or other benefit and whether it is in accordance with\r\nthe dividend or bonus policy. The directors must consider the actuary\xe2\x80\x99s report before declaring\r\nthe dividend, bonus or other benefit on participating policies (464(2)).

\r\n\r\n

The AA is required to provide the company with a written opinion on whether the method\r\nselected for allocating investment income or losses and expenses to the participating account is\r\nfair and equitable to the participating policyholders (457, 458). The company must file a copy of\r\nthe AA\xe2\x80\x99s written opinion with the Superintendent (459), together with a description of the\r\nallocation method.

\r\n\r\n

Each year, the AA must report, in writing, to the directors on the fairness and equitableness of\r\nthe allocation method used by the company (460). The AA is also required to report on whether\r\npayment to shareholders or a transfer to an account from which payments can be made to\r\nshareholders from the profits of the participating account would materially affect the company\xe2\x80\x99s\r\nability to comply with its dividend or bonus policy or to maintain the level of dividends paid to\r\nparticipating policyholders (461(c)).

\r\n\r\n

The directors of a company must establish criteria for changes made by the company to the\r\npremium or charge for insurance, amount of insurance or surrender value in respect of its\r\nadjustable policies (165(2)(e.2)). The AA is required to report to the directors in writing on the\r\nfairness to adjustable policyholders of the criteria established or amended under 165(2)(e.2)) and\r\nreport at least once during each financial year on their continuing fairness (165(3.3)).

\r\n\r\n

The AA is required to report, annually and in writing, to the directors on whether the changes the\r\ncompany made in respect of its adjustable policies during the preceding 12 months are in\r\naccordance with the criteria established under paragraph 165(2)(e.2) and are fair to the adjustable\r\npolicyholders (464.1(1)).

\r\n\r\n

Additional requirements and guidance concerning participating accounts can be found in the\r\nPolicyholders Disclosure Regulations and OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s Guideline E-16 Participating Account\r\nManagement and Disclosure to Participating and Adjustable Policyholders.

\r\n\r\n

For insurance companies, OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s Guideline Minimum Continuing Capital and Surplus\r\nRequirements (MCCSR) and the Test of Adequacy of Assets in Canada and Margin\r\nRequirements (TAAM) sets out requirements for the test of capital adequacy (515, 608). The\r\nMCCSR and TAAM returns require the AA\xe2\x80\x99s confirmation that the instructions pertaining both\r\nto the MCCSR and TAAM Guidelines and to the annual return have been followed. OSFI also\r\nexpects an opinion signed by the AA and a memorandum, both covering the areas where the\r\ncalculation required discretion or where significant technical calculations, methodologies and\r\njudgements were applied.

\r\n\r\n

Section 2: Qualifications Required

\r\n\r\n

As previously noted, the AA is required to be an FCIA. The AA is, therefore, subject to the\r\nCIA\xe2\x80\x99s Rules of Professional Conduct. Rule 1 requires the AA to act honestly and to perform\r\nprofessional services with integrity, competence, skill and care. Rule 2 requires a CIA member\r\nto perform professional services only when the member is qualified to do so and meets\r\napplicable qualification standards. Professional services performed by a CIA member must,\r\nunder Rule 3, meet applicable standards of practice.

\r\n\r\n

The Superintendent may disqualify or remove a senior officer if the Superintendent is of the\r\nopinion that the senior officer is not suitable to hold the position (678.1(4) and 678.2(1)).

\r\n\r\n

In assessing the suitability of an AA, the Superintendent expects that the AA has each of the\r\nfollowing qualifications:

\r\n\r\n
    \r\n

  1. has appropriate Canadian practical experience, which is defined as Work in CanadaFootnote 5 for\r\nat least three of the last six years, of which at least one year was performing valuation of\r\nCanadian actuarial liabilities of an insurance company;

    2. \r\n

  2. has experience with the CIA\xe2\x80\x99s Standards of Practice and relevant insurance legislation\r\nand regulation;

    3. \r\n

  3. is up to date with respect to the CIA\xe2\x80\x99s Continuing Professional Development\r\nrequirement;

    4. \r\n

  4. has not been the subject of an adverse finding by a CIA Disciplinary Tribunal. Where\r\nthere has been such a finding, the Superintendent may nevertheless conclude that the AA\r\nis a suitable person if the circumstances of the case and other information support such a\r\nconclusion.

    5. \r\n
\r\n\r\n

Section 3: Peer Review of the Work of the Actuary

\r\n\r\n

a. Background

\r\n\r\n

OSFI believes that regular peer review of certain work performed by the AA is of significant\r\nbenefit both to OSFI and to a company\xe2\x80\x99s stakeholders by contributing to the safety and\r\nsoundness of insurance companies, as described in the general objectives below. In addition,\r\nregular peer review is also of benefit to the AA by providing a source of independent advice and\r\na means of consulting with other knowledgeable actuaries. Consequently, OSFI expects that all\r\nfederally regulated insurance companies will appoint peer reviewers to implement peer review\r\nprocesses consistent with the OSFI criteria described below. The peer review should be\r\nconducted in accordance with accepted actuarial practice as specified in the CIA\xe2\x80\x99s Standards of\r\nPractice, in particular with section 1640 of these standards and any related educational notes.

\r\n\r\n

b. General Objectives

\r\n\r\n

In requiring peer review of the work of the AA, OSFI has the following objectives:

\r\n\r\n
    \r\n

  • assist OSFI in its assessment of the insurer\xe2\x80\x99s safety and soundness: The AA has the\r\nresponsibility for the valuation of policy liabilities in the financial statements and future\r\nfinancial condition reporting. Actuarial peer review is one tool OSFI uses in its\r\nassessment of the safety and soundness of insurers.

    • \r\n

  • be of benefit to the AA by providing (i) a source of independent consultation advice, and\r\n(ii) an additional source of professional education: This aids in narrowing the range of\r\npractice by AA\xe2\x80\x99s and improving the quality of their work. OSFI recognizes that this is\r\nnot the only source of professional development for the AA.

    • \r\n

  • maintain and strengthen confidence in the work of the AA by the public, by insurance\r\ncompany management and directors and by supervisory authorities.

    • \r\n
\r\n\r\n

It is OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s intention to periodically meet with the peer reviewer to discuss the report and the\r\nfindings of a review. It is also OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s intention to periodically meet with the AA to discuss the\r\nvalue being obtained by the peer review process.

\r\n\r\n

c. Work to be Reviewed

\r\n\r\n

In order to assist in achieving OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s objective to assess insurers\xe2\x80\x99 safety and soundness, the\r\nreviewer is expected to:

\r\n\r\n
    \r\n

  1. ascertain that the work of the AA for the valuation of policy liabilities and ceded\r\nreinsurance assets is in compliance with accepted actuarial practice, as established by the\r\nActuarial Standards Board and the CIA, and is consistent with any objectives or\r\nrequirements established by OSFI in Regulations, Guidelines or the Memorandum to the\r\nAA (Note that the peer review work is not intended to duplicate the work of the external\r\nauditor. See Section 3d below.);

    2. \r\n

  2. review the appropriateness and extent of internal and external material changes affecting\r\nthe valuation of policy liabilities and ceded reinsurance assets. The reviewer should\r\nassess the risk of material misstatement or omissions arising from each change, as\r\nopposed to just the net effect of off-setting changes.

    3. \r\n

  3. review the adequacy of procedures, systems and the work of others relied on by the AA,\r\nto the extent that these are not reviewed by the external auditor. This includes checks on\r\ndata integrity and checks on procedures and methodologies used to validate the valuation\r\ncalculations and results;

    4. \r\n

  4. discuss with the AA the appropriateness of each of the assumptions used and the methods\r\nemployed in the valuation of actuarial policy liabilities and ascertain that the assumptions\r\nare at the appropriate point in the range of accepted actuarial practice, given the\r\ncircumstances of the company;

    5. \r\n

  5. determine whether the Appointed Actuary\xe2\x80\x99s Report (AAR) sufficiently describes the\r\nvaluation assumptions and valuation methodology employed by the AA;

    6. \r\n

  6. for life insurance companies\xe2\x80\x99 MCCSR/TAAM returns, and for P&C companies\xe2\x80\x99\r\nMCT/BAAT returns, review the work of the AA in the areas that require actuarial\r\nassumptions and calculations and ensure the work is consistent with the report, if any,\r\naccompanying these filings;

    7. \r\n

  7. review and discuss with the AA the methodology, assumptions and scenarios used for\r\nfuture financial condition reporting as required by the Superintendent in Section 1 of this\r\nGuideline, usually based on Dynamic Capital Adequacy Testing (DCAT); and

    8. \r\n

  8. produce a written report(s) documenting the findings of the peer review.

    9. \r\n
\r\n\r\n

The AA and management of the company should co-operate fully with the peer reviewer when\r\nthe review is being carried out. Best efforts should be used to provide the reviewer with access\r\nto any required documents and to provide any additional explanations that may be relevant to the\r\npeer review.

\r\n\r\n

Examples of material changes referenced in point 2 above include, but are not limited to:

\r\n\r\n
    \r\n

  • changes in key actuarial valuation assumptions or unusual adverse or favourable loss\r\ndevelopment;

    • \r\n

  • changes in methodology used in the valuation;

    • \r\n

  • changes in company operations or circumstances (e.g. acquisitions, investment policy,\r\netc.);

    • \r\n

  • use of a revised valuation model (e.g. the review should cover general methodology, but\r\ndoes not need to be a full software audit or a recalculation.);

    • \r\n

  • material events that would suggest that valuation assumptions or methods may need to be\r\nmodified (e.g. fundamental economic changes, changes in corporate legal structure,\r\ntaxation law changes, material new line of business, etc.). For such events, a review\r\nshould also be included of the valuation assumptions and methods for material blocks of\r\nbusiness that are subject to high sensitivity, but where no changes have been made.

    • \r\n
\r\n\r\n

d. Materiality Considerations

\r\n\r\n

The materiality level used for a company\xe2\x80\x99s financial statements is set by the external auditor\r\nbased on the size of the company as a whole. For the purpose of peer review, both the reviewer\r\nand the AA should follow the description of materiality as contained in the CIA standards of\r\npractice. This requires materiality to be set from the point of view of the prime user of the work.\r\nAs such, OSFI expects the materiality level for peer review to be appropriate at the line of\r\nbusiness level at which the assumptions are set.

\r\n\r\n

Materiality is a matter of professional judgement in the particular circumstances. While auditors\r\nmay determine quantitative materiality for audit purposes at the total company level, materiality\r\nfor the purposes of peer review cannot be determined solely by means of the application of a\r\nnumeric threshold. General quantitative guidelines are not a substitute for the AA\xe2\x80\x99s and peer\r\nreviewer\xe2\x80\x99s professional judgement.

\r\n\r\n

Materiality should become more rigorous as the company approaches any internal capital targets\r\nor regulatory capital thresholds.

\r\n\r\n

e. Peer Review and External Audit

\r\n\r\n

The Canadian Institute of Chartered Accountants (CICA) and the Canadian Institute of Actuaries\r\n(CIA) have issued a paper entitled Guide: Audits of Financial Statements That Contain Amounts\r\nThat Have Been Determined Using Actuarial Calculations (the CICA Guide). This paper\r\nprovides guidance to external auditors for applying the requirements of the Canadian Auditing\r\nStandards (CASs). These standards state that the overall objective of the auditor is to obtain\r\nreasonable assurance about whether the financial statements as a whole are free from material\r\nmisstatement.

\r\n\r\n

However, OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s objective and scope for peer review is to assess the safety and soundness of\r\ninsurers by reviewing the AA\xe2\x80\x99s work for the financial statements at a more granular level. It is\r\nOSFI\xe2\x80\x99s view that each of the assumptions used should be independently reasonable, in accord\r\nwith accepted actuarial standards, and that the methodology should be appropriate for each\r\nvaluation model. OSFI expects the peer reviewer to express an opinion on the appropriateness of\r\nthe policy liabilities at this more granular level and to provide feedback to the AA on the various\r\naspects of their work. Therefore, since the objective of an external audit differs from the\r\nobjective of a peer review, the audit work done to satisfy the audit requirements may not be\r\nsufficient to fully address the peer review requirements under this Guideline.

\r\n\r\n

It is not OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s intention that the requirements for the peer review work duplicate the work of\r\nthe external auditor, including any actuary assisting the auditor. The peer reviewer is not\r\nrequired to perform any detailed recalculations, as long as the reviewer determines that the\r\ncontrols and procedures used by the AA are adequate to identify potential errors in the valuation\r\nresults. The peer reviewer is also not required to verify data or controls.

\r\n\r\n

Where the auditor\xe2\x80\x99s actuarial specialist on the engagement team is not an FCIA, the peer\r\nreviewer should take extra care to verify that all CIA standards are met by the AA.

\r\n\r\n

f. Contents of Peer Review Reports

\r\n\r\n

OSFI expects the peer reviewer to prepare a report documenting the findings of the reviews.

\r\n\r\n

In the case of a Canadian or provincial insurance company, the peer reviewer\xe2\x80\x99s written report, or\r\na summary of it, is expected to be submitted to the audit committee of the company\xe2\x80\x99s board of\r\ndirectors at the meeting subsequent to the completion of the report. In the case of a Canadian\r\nbranch of a foreign insurance company, the report is to be submitted to the Chief Agent. In both\r\ncases, the full report and any summary report are to be submitted to OSFI.

\r\n\r\n

The full peer review written report should include the following:

\r\n\r\n
    \r\n
  • a description of the work done by the reviewer (both the specifics of the work and its\r\nextent);
    • \r\n
  • the timing during the year when the work was carried out;
    • \r\n
  • the materiality level used for the review;
    • \r\n
  • the reviewer\xe2\x80\x99s statement of opinion with respect to the AA\xe2\x80\x99s compliance with accepted\r\nactuarial practice and any objectives or requirements established by OSFI in Regulations,\r\nGuidelines or Memoranda to the AA;
    • \r\n
  • the reviewer\xe2\x80\x99s observations with respect to changes made in methodology and assumptions;
    • \r\n
  • the reviewer\xe2\x80\x99s acknowledgement that no additional material changes should have been\r\nmade;
    • \r\n
  • a list of any recommendations for further review or work by the AA in the coming year;\r\nand
    • \r\n
  • a brief description of the relationship with the AA to support the specific objectives of\r\nproviding consultation aid, professional education and improving the quality of the AA\xe2\x80\x99s\r\nwork.
    • \r\n
\r\n\r\n

g. Peer Review Cycle

\r\n\r\n

Each item of the AA\xe2\x80\x99s work described in subsection 3(c) above dealing with the financial\r\nstatements should be reviewed and reported on at least once every three years, either all at once\r\nor in phases over a three-year cycle.

\r\n\r\n

However, OSFI expects material changes (see subsection 3.c.2), if any, affecting the valuation of\r\npolicy liabilities or ceded reinsurance assets to be reviewed and reported on annually. If there\r\nare no material changes, and the reviewer is in agreement that this is appropriate, the reviewer\r\nshould still prepare and file a brief report to that effect.

\r\n\r\n\r\n

A full review of the financial condition reporting (subsection 3.c.7) is expected to be prepared\r\nevery three years. A limited annual review is only required to address the appropriateness of the\r\nscenarios employed. The peer reviewer is expected to prepare reports documenting the findings\r\nof both the full 3-year review and the limited annual review.

\r\n\r\n

h. Timing of the Peer Review Work and Reports

\r\n\r\n

Due to the separate timing during the year of the AA\xe2\x80\x99s work for a company\xe2\x80\x99s financial\r\nstatements and for financial condition reporting, there can be more than one report.

\r\n\r\n

With regard to the financial statement related work (items c.1, c.2, c.3, c.4 and c.6 above), OSFI\r\nencourages the peer review to be pre-release (i.e. carried out prior to the release of the AA\r\nreporting on the statement related work).

\r\n\r\n

The review of the AAR (item c.5 above) can be post-release.

\r\n\r\n

The review of the future financial condition reports is also encouraged to be pre-release, but may\r\nbe post-release depending on the circumstances of the company.

\r\n\r\n

To qualify as a pre-release peer review, the reviewer must prepare the report and sign his/her\r\nopinion on, or shortly before, the date the AA reports on any work. For example, the filing of\r\nthe Life-1, Life-2, P&C-1 or P&C-2 statements with OSFI is deemed to be AA reporting. For a\r\npre-release peer review of work subject to external audit, the peer review report should be\r\nsubmitted to the audit committee or to the Chief Agent on, or shortly before, the date the AA\r\nreports on any work.

\r\n\r\n

The complete peer review reports, and any summaries, are expected to be submitted to OSFI on a\r\nconfidential basis. Copies of pre-release reports, both the full peer review report, and any\r\nsummary, for financial statement work should be forwarded to OSFI based on the same\r\ndeadlines that apply to filings of the Life-1, Life-2, P&C-1 or P&C-2 reports. For post-release\r\nreviews, the reviewer\xe2\x80\x99s report should be submitted to OSFI no later than thirty days after release\r\nof the AA\xe2\x80\x99s report on the work reviewed, and for future financial condition reports, no later than\r\nDecember 31.

\r\n\r\n

If a member or employee of the insurer\xe2\x80\x99s external audit firm peer reviews work by the AA that is\r\nsubject to audit, OSFI expects that such a peer review will be completed prior to the issuance of\r\nthe audit opinion.

\r\n\r\n

i. Selecting a Reviewer

\r\n\r\n

OSFI expects each company to hire a single peer reviewer for the company as a whole. If there\r\nare affiliate or subsidiary companies within one group, a single peer reviewer should be\r\nresponsible for assessment of the entire group of companies, including the preparation of the\r\npeer review reports and opinions. The reviewer may, however, engage other sub-reviewers to\r\ntake advantage of particular competencies.

\r\n\r\n

A peer reviewer is expected to meet the same qualification standards as are outlined in Section 2\r\nof this Guideline with respect to the AA, including the Superintendent\xe2\x80\x99s minimum requirements\r\nfor suitability.

\r\n\r\n

It is good practice for the audit committee of the company\xe2\x80\x99s board of directors, or the chief agent\r\nin the case of a foreign company, to be advised of the terms of the peer review and the selection\r\nof the reviewer before the review is undertaken.

\r\n\r\n

OSFI expects a reviewer to have sufficient experience with respect to the type of work to be\r\nreviewed. The reviewer\xe2\x80\x99s prior experience should include exposure to two or more unrelated\r\ninsurance companies in order that the reviewer be familiar with the range of practices and\r\nassumptions used by actuaries in Canada. Knowledge of industry best practices is needed to\r\nensure the review process appropriately fulfils its educational and consultative objectives.

\r\n\r\n

OSFI expects a company to notify it in writing forthwith after hiring the peer reviewer, and to\r\nprovide the reasons for any change in reviewer.

\r\n\r\n

It is essential to the integrity of the peer review process that a reviewer be, and be seen to be,\r\nobjective. The reviewer should, therefore, have no relationship with the insurer or with the AA\r\nthat would in any way impair objectivity. The reviewer is expected to follow the CIA Standards\r\nof Practice as well as any additional OSFI requirements. In practice, without limiting the\r\ngenerality of the foregoing, OSFI believes that the following criteria should be applied in\r\ndetermining the objectivity of a reviewer:

\r\n\r\n
    \r\n

  • A reviewer may not be an employee of the company or any affiliated companies, and\r\nmay not have been employed by the company or served as AA of the company during the\r\nthree years prior to the date of the work being reviewed;

    • \r\n

  • A reviewer must not be a shareholder of, or have a direct financial investment (other than\r\nas a policyholder, depositor, beneficiary or insured) in the company;

    • \r\n

  • A reviewer may have an indirect interest (e.g. through a diversified mutual fund\r\ninvestment) in the company;

    • \r\n

  • If a member of a consulting firm is the AA, another member of the same firm may not be\r\nthe peer reviewer;

    • \r\n

  • If a member of a consulting firm is involved in any actuarial work related to the financial\r\nstatements or financial condition reporting for the company, another member of the same\r\nfirm may be the peer reviewer only if he/she is not involved in this work for the\r\ncompany. In this context, \xe2\x80\x9cactuarial work\xe2\x80\x9d includes deciding on methodology, selecting\r\nassumptions and producing results.

    • \r\n

  • It is acceptable, and in fact expected, that the AA will be in contact with the peer\r\nreviewer during the course of the year to discuss the potential acceptability of changes in\r\nmethodologies and assumptions that the AA is considering. However, the reviewer should only provide advice with respect to these changes as part of the peer review work.\r\nThis is similar to the interaction of a company with its external auditor.

    • \r\n

  • A peer reviewer may be an actuary working in the company\xe2\x80\x99s external audit firm, but\r\ncompanies are encouraged to not use as a peer reviewer an actuarial specialist who is a\r\nmember of the audit team for the company.

    • \r\n
\r\n\r\n

OSFI regards an actuary working for the external audit firm to be sufficiently independent to be a\r\npeer reviewer. Using an actuary from the external audit firm can accommodate smaller and\r\nsimpler companies. Notwithstanding this, OSFI expects large and complex companies to engage\r\na peer reviewer who is not a member of its external audit firm. While OSFI recognizes that\r\nexternal audit firms are independent, it is of the view that a separate independent actuarial peer\r\nreview is desirable since it will give additional perspective to large and complex companies.

\r\n\r\n

However, as noted in subsection 3(h), if an actuary in the external audit firm\xe2\x80\x99s actuary is used for\r\npeer review, the peer review of any work that is subject to audit should be completed prior to the\r\nissuance of the audit opinion. In addition, this peer review work should be performed under a\r\nstand-alone engagement that is separate from the audit engagement.

\r\n\r\n

Note that the criteria for objectivity for peer review purposes are not as restrictive as those found\r\nin OSFI\xe2\x80\x99s Guideline E-14, Role of the Independent Actuary (i.e., rules required for\r\namalgamations of companies or buying/selling of blocks of business). In the latter case, the\r\nIndependent Actuary represents, in the transaction, policyholders who rely on the Independent\r\nActuary. In the case of peer review, OSFI, policyholders, management and shareholders\r\ncontinue to depend on the AA.

\r\n\r\n

j. Changing a Reviewer

\r\n\r\n

To enhance the peer reviewer\xe2\x80\x99s objectivity and increase the educational value of the review\r\nprocess, a regular change or rotation of reviewers is expected. This allows the AA to obtain\r\ndifferent perspectives. Therefore, OSFI expects that a reviewer will be changed at least once\r\nevery two cycles (i.e., every six years). However, the company has the option of making more\r\nfrequent changes.

\r\n\r\n

If a peer reviewer is a member of a consulting or audit firm, another member of the same firm\r\nmay be acceptable as a new peer reviewer. In this case, a previous reviewer may be reappointed\r\nto this role after a period of at least one cycle (i.e., at least three years).

\r\n\r\n

When a company changes its peer reviewer, it should notify OSFI in writing forthwith stating the\r\nreasons for the change.

\r\n\r\n\r\n\r\n
